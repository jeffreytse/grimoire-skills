# Apply Stock-Making Fundamentals

Produce a clear, deeply flavored stock by correctly preparing bones, controlling extraction temperature, skimming actively, and finishing with proper straining — the foundational technique that separates professional cooking from home cooking.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Classical French cuisine (Auguste Escoffier) codified stock-making as the foundation of all sauces — the five mother sauces and their derivatives are all built on stocks. Culinary Institute of America (CIA), Le Cordon Bleu, and every classical European culinary program make stock production a first-week fundamental. Thomas Keller's "The French Laundry Cookbook" dedicates an entire section to the philosophy that quality stocks cannot be replaced by commercial broth.
**Impact:** A well-made stock is the single highest-leverage investment in kitchen output — one 8-hour stock batch produces the flavor base for dozens of dishes. Commercial broth is high in sodium and MSG, masked in flavor, and gelatinous only from additives; a properly made bone stock contains natural collagen-derived gelatin that produces body and mouthfeel impossible to replicate commercially.

## Steps

### 1. Select and prepare the bones

Quality bones determine the final flavor:
- **Chicken stock:** raw carcasses, backs, necks, wings; roast for brown stock (roast at 450°F/230°C until golden before simmering), skip roasting for white (fond blanc)
- **Beef/veal stock:** knuckle bones, marrow bones, feet (high in collagen); always roast for fond brun
- **Fish stock (fumet):** fish frames and heads (non-oily fish: sole, halibut, snapper); never roast; maximum 20–30 min simmer (longer = bitter)
- **Vegetable stock:** no bones; onion, carrot, celery, leek, mushroom stems, tomato

**Blanch raw beef/veal bones first:** cover with cold water, bring to a boil, discard water — removes blood and impurities that would cloud the stock.

Ratio: 3–4 lbs bones per gallon (4L) of water.

### 2. Build the aromatic base (mirepoix)

Standard mirepoix ratio: **2 parts onion : 1 part carrot : 1 part celery** by weight.

For 1 gallon stock: ~1 lb (450g) total mirepoix.

Additional aromatics (bouquet garni):
- Bay leaves, thyme, parsley stems, peppercorns — tie in cheesecloth or leek greens
- Tomato paste (for brown stock only): adds color and acidity
- Leek: adds depth; optional but recommended

Do not add: brassica vegetables (cabbage, broccoli), starchy vegetables (potato), or strong spices — these create off-flavors or cloud the stock.

### 3. Control temperature — never boil

Critical step most beginners miss:

**Target temperature: 180–195°F (82–90°C)** — a bare simmer with small bubbles barely breaking the surface.

Boiling causes:
- Protein and fat emulsification → permanently cloudy stock
- Fat breaks into fine droplets → greasy mouthfeel
- Overcooking of delicate proteins → harsh, flat flavor

Use a thermometer to verify. Maintain this temperature for:
- Chicken: 3–4 hours
- Beef/veal: 6–8 hours
- Fish fumet: 20–30 minutes
- Vegetable: 45–60 minutes

### 4. Skim actively during the first hour

In the first 30–60 minutes, grey foam and fat rise to the surface — these are coagulated proteins and impurities that cloud the stock:
- Skim every 10–15 minutes with a ladle or fine-mesh spoon
- Do not stir (stirring emulsifies the foam back in)
- Add a splash of cold water if needed to encourage foam to rise

Once skimming yields clear foam (small white bubbles = fine), skimming frequency can reduce.

### 5. Strain, degrease, and store

**Straining:**
1. Ladle stock gently through a fine-mesh strainer or cheesecloth-lined strainer
2. Do not press solids (pressing releases clouding particles)
3. Discard bones and aromatics

**Degreasing:**
- Hot method: use a ladle or fat separator immediately
- Cold method: refrigerate overnight; lift solid fat layer off the top (produces clearer result)

**Reducing and storing:**
- Reduce to concentrate flavor if storage space is limited (can reduce 4:1 to "glace")
- Cool rapidly in an ice bath before refrigerating (food safety; target below 40°F within 2 hours)
- Refrigerator: 4–5 days; Freezer: 3–6 months; ice cube trays for small portions

### 6. Evaluate quality — the gel test

A properly made stock should gel when cold (from natural gelatin extracted from collagen):
- Refrigerate a small amount; it should become firm or semi-firm
- Weak gel = under-extracted or insufficient collagen; simmer longer or add more collagen-rich bones (feet, knuckles)
- A stock that gels properly produces superior body and mouthfeel in finished sauces

## Common Mistakes

- **Starting with hot water:** Always start bones in cold water — cold water draws impurities out slowly, producing cleaner stock. Hot water seizes proteins and traps impurities inside.
- **Boiling instead of simmering:** A rolling boil permanently clouds the stock and cannot be corrected. Maintain a gentle simmer throughout.
- **Adding salt:** Do not salt stocks — they will be reduced into sauces where salt concentrates. Season at the sauce stage, not the stock stage.

## When NOT to Use

- Quick pan sauce immediately after searing: use the pan drippings directly with wine, vinegar, and butter — deglazing is faster than stock production. Reserve stock-making for batch production, not single-meal applications.
