# Calculate Baker's Percentage

Express all baking ingredients as a percentage of total flour weight so recipes can be scaled, compared, and replicated precisely regardless of batch size.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** All professional bakeries; King Arthur Flour professional baking program; Culinary Institute of America baking curriculum; Jeffrey Hamelman's foundational bread curriculum; every artisan and commercial bread production operation.
**Impact:** Baker's percentage enables infinite batch scaling with zero conversion errors; professional bakers using weight-based percentages report ~90% reduction in recipe failures compared to volume-based measurement; a recipe expressed in baker's percentage can scale from a 500g home loaf to a 500kg commercial run using identical ratios and identical results.
**Why best:** Volume measurement is inaccurate for baking — a cup of flour varies by 20-30% by weight depending on how it is scooped. Baker's percentage eliminates this variability by anchoring everything to flour weight. It also makes hydration immediately readable (a 75% hydration dough is wetter than a 65% dough) without needing to calculate ratios separately.

Sources: Hamelman J "Bread: A Baker's Book of Techniques and Recipes" 2nd ed. (2004), Chapter 1; King Arthur Flour Professional Baking Guide; CIA "Baking and Pastry: Mastering the Art and Craft" (3rd ed.).

## Steps

1. **Weigh all ingredients** — Convert every ingredient from volume to weight using a calibrated kitchen scale. Never start with volume measures; if a recipe only gives cups, convert to grams first using standard density tables.

2. **Identify total flour weight** — Total flour is always 100% in baker's percentage. If a recipe uses multiple flours (bread flour + whole wheat + rye), sum all flour weights — that sum is 100%.

3. **Calculate each ingredient's percentage** — Divide each ingredient's weight by total flour weight, then multiply by 100.
   Formula: `(Ingredient weight ÷ Total flour weight) × 100 = Baker's %`
   Example: 750g water ÷ 1000g flour × 100 = 75% hydration.

4. **Calculate hydration percentage first** — Hydration (total water weight ÷ total flour weight × 100) is the single most important ratio. It determines dough strength, crumb structure, and handling. Ciabatta: ~75-80%; baguette: ~65-68%; bagel: ~55-60%.

5. **Express salt as a percentage** — Standard bread salt range is 1.8-2.2% of flour weight. If the recipe shows less than 1.5% or more than 2.5%, check for an error. This sanity check catches common recipe transcription mistakes.

6. **Express yeast or starter as a percentage** — Commercial yeast typically 0.5-2%; sourdough levain typically 10-25%. These ranges are calibration checks — outside them, fermentation timing will be dramatically different from the recipe's intent.

7. **Scale the recipe** — To change batch size: choose new total flour weight → multiply each percentage by the new flour weight ÷ 100 to get new ingredient weights.
   Example: Scale 1000g flour recipe to 1500g flour: multiply every ingredient weight by 1.5.

8. **Verify the formula totals** — Sum all baker's percentages. A standard bread formula totals 160-180% (100% flour + 65-75% water + 2% salt + 1-2% yeast + remaining enrichments). Significant deviations indicate a missing ingredient or error.

9. **Adjust for pre-ferment or soaker** — If the recipe uses a poolish, biga, or sourdough starter, track flour and water within those elements as part of total flour and water. Pre-ferment flour contributes to the 100% flour total; its water adjusts total hydration.

10. **Document in baker's percentage format** — Record every recipe exclusively in weight and baker's percentage. Never record in volume. The percentage format is the master document; weights are derived when needed.

## Rules

- Total flour is always 100% — never assign 100% to the largest ingredient if it is not flour.
- All percentages are relative to flour weight only, not total dough weight — this is the most common error when people confuse baker's % with standard percentage calculations.
- Weigh every ingredient including water, eggs, and salt — volume measurement is not acceptable for professional or repeatable baking.
- When using multiple flours, combine all into a single 100% base before calculating other percentages.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using total dough weight as the 100% base** — this produces percentages that look similar but are mathematically incorrect and cannot be used for scaling; flour is always 100%.
- **Forgetting flour in the pre-ferment** — if 200g of the 1000g flour is in a poolish, only 800g remains for the final dough; the full 1000g still counts as 100% but the split must be tracked.
- **Calculating hydration from water alone** — eggs, milk, and other wet ingredients contribute to effective hydration; for enriched doughs, account for all water-containing ingredients.
- **Rounding aggressively** — in large batches, even 1% rounding error at each ingredient compounds to significant weight differences; carry percentages to one decimal place minimum.

## When NOT to Use

- Pastry recipes where egg, butter, and sugar drive ratio logic differently (pâte sablée, croissants, choux) — pastry percentage systems reference total dough weight or use distinct ratio frameworks.
- Simple home recipes used once without scaling or comparison needs — baker's percentage adds overhead not justified by single-use recipes.
- Recipes without flour as the primary structural ingredient (meringue, candy, chocolate tempering) where a different base ingredient drives the ratio.
