# Design Argument Structure

Design a logical argument structure for academic or policy writing — developing a clear thesis, supporting with appropriate evidence, articulating logical warrants, and addressing counterarguments — to produce a persuasive, well-reasoned written argument.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Stephen Toulmin's "The Uses of Argument" (1958) — Toulmin's model of argumentation (claim, data, warrant, backing, rebuttal, qualifier) is the standard framework in academic argumentation, debate coaching, and rhetoric instruction at universities worldwide. Booth, Colomb & Williams' "The Craft of Research" is the primary academic writing methodology text used at US universities. Graff & Birkenstein's "They Say / I Say" has been adopted at over 1,500 institutions and is the most widely used academic writing pedagogy text.
**Impact:** Undisciplined arguments have three common failures: theses that are too broad to be argued; evidence without explicit logical connections to the claim; and omission of counterarguments that a reader will raise. Each failure undermines the argument's credibility with an academic audience. The Toulmin framework addresses all three by requiring explicit articulation of the claim, the supporting data, and — crucially — the warrant that connects the data to the claim.

## Steps

### 1. Develop a specific, arguable thesis

The thesis is the claim the argument is designed to prove — the one sentence that the entire paper supports:
- **Arguable:** the thesis should be contestable; someone with access to the same information could reasonably disagree
- **Specific:** broad theses ("climate change is a serious problem") are not arguable because no one disagrees; narrow theses ("carbon capture at current cost is not the most cost-effective emissions reduction strategy available") are arguable and directable
- **Assertive:** the thesis takes a position; questions, summaries, or observations are not theses

**The thesis test:**
- Can you explain the thesis in one clear sentence?
- Can you imagine a well-informed, reasonable person disagreeing?
- Does the thesis make a claim that requires evidence to support?

**Development process:** start with an observation ("I notice that carbon capture receives a large share of climate funding") → find the complication ("but I also see research suggesting other strategies are more cost-effective") → form the thesis ("current investment in carbon capture is disproportionate to its cost-effectiveness relative to alternative emissions reduction strategies")

### 2. Structure evidence by type and arrange for maximum persuasive force

Not all evidence is equal; academic arguments use multiple evidence types:
- **Empirical evidence:** research data, experimental results, statistics, documented cases; the strongest evidence type for empirical claims
- **Expert authority:** citation of recognized authorities in the field; strong when the authority is directly relevant; weak when the expert is outside their domain
- **Logical inference:** conclusions drawn through deductive or inductive reasoning; strong when the premises are established and the logic is valid
- **Analogy:** comparison to a similar case; useful for illustration but not sufficient as sole support for a claim

**Evidence arrangement strategies:**
- **Strongest to weakest:** place the most compelling evidence first; readers remember the beginning and may not reach the end
- **Weakest to strongest:** build to the most compelling evidence; suitable for oral arguments where the climactic structure produces impact
- **Alternating strong/weak:** intersperse strong evidence with explanatory material; maintains engagement while supporting the argument

**Evidence-claim connection:** each piece of evidence requires an explicit connection to the claim. "The data shows X" followed by "Therefore, [thesis claim]" is insufficient without the warrant: "This matters because [the principle that connects X to the claim]."

### 3. Articulate logical warrants

The warrant is the logical principle that connects evidence to claim — the most frequently missing element in student arguments:
- **Implicit warrant (common error):** presenting evidence and claiming it supports the thesis without explaining why
- **Explicit warrant:** "Research shows carbon capture costs $100/ton of CO₂ avoided [data]. Since cost-effectiveness per ton of CO₂ is the appropriate metric for comparing climate investments [warrant], carbon capture is less cost-effective than afforestation at $3–50/ton [comparison data]. Therefore, current investment levels in carbon capture are disproportionate [claim]."

**Testing warrants:**
- Is the warrant a generally accepted principle or does it itself require defense?
- Does the warrant actually connect this specific data to this specific claim?
- Could a reasonable person accept the data but reject the claim by rejecting the warrant?

If the warrant is controversial: defend it before using it to support the main claim.

### 4. Address the strongest counterargument

Booth, Colomb & Williams and Graff & Birkenstein both identify counterargument engagement as the mark of a mature academic argument. An argument that ignores counterarguments appears either unaware of them or afraid of them.

**They Say / I Say structure (Graff & Birkenstein):**
1. Steel-man the opposing view: "Proponents of carbon capture argue that [the strongest form of their argument]"
2. Acknowledge what is true in the counterargument: "This is true insofar as..."
3. Pivot with a concessive rebuttal: "However, this view fails to account for..."
4. Return to and reinforce your claim

**The steel-man principle:** engage with the strongest version of the counterargument, not a strawman; academic audiences will recognize the difference; engaging with a weak version of the opposition implies you haven't encountered (or cannot address) the strong version.

### 5. Design the argument's architecture before writing

Before drafting, map the argument's structure:
```
Thesis: [specific, arguable claim]

Supporting argument 1:
- Claim: 
- Evidence: 
- Warrant: 

Supporting argument 2:
- Claim: 
- Evidence: 
- Warrant: 

Supporting argument 3:
- Claim: 
- Evidence: 
- Warrant: 

Counterargument: [strongest opposition]
- Their claim:
- Their evidence:
- My rebuttal: 
- Why my thesis holds: 

Conclusion: [thesis + implications]
```

This architecture map reveals structural weaknesses before they are embedded in prose:
- Does each supporting claim actually support the main thesis?
- Are there gaps where evidence is asserted but not presented?
- Is the counterargument actually addressed or just acknowledged?

## Common Mistakes

- **Thesis that summarizes rather than argues:** "This paper will discuss the effects of carbon capture on climate change" is a roadmap, not a thesis; a thesis is a claim, not a description of what the paper contains
- **Evidence without explicit warrant:** assuming the connection between evidence and claim is obvious; academic readers test every connection; make the connection explicit
- **Strawmanning the opposition:** engaging with a weakened version of the counterargument; a well-informed reader who holds the counterargument view will notice and discount the rebuttal

## When NOT to Use

- Exploratory or analytical writing (literature reviews, analytical essays that describe without arguing): not all academic writing makes a claim; some academic forms (annotated bibliography, literature synthesis, case study description) organize and analyze without arguing a thesis; these forms have different structural requirements than argument-based writing.
