# Apply AIDA

Structure persuasive copy using the Attention-Interest-Desire-Action sequence to guide readers from awareness to committed action.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Direct Marketing Association; advertising agencies globally including Ogilvy, BBDO, and Leo Burnett; email marketing platforms including Mailchimp and HubSpot as curriculum content

**Impact:** AIDA-structured direct mail historically outperforms unstructured copy by 20-40% in response rate (DMA Annual Report data); Ogilvy's AIDA-based campaigns for American Express and Rolls-Royce set industry benchmarks still studied today

**Why best:** AIDA works because it maps to the reader's cognitive journey. You cannot demand action from a reader who is not yet interested. You cannot generate desire in a reader who is not yet interested. The sequence is not arbitrary — each stage is a prerequisite for the next.

## Steps

1. **Attention — interrupt the scroll** — open with a pattern interrupt: a provocative question, a striking statistic, a counterintuitive claim, or a specific scenario the target reader recognizes as their own life
2. **Interest — earn the next sentence** — transition from the hook to the problem or opportunity with specificity; show you understand the reader's context better than they expected
3. **Desire — stack the case** — paint the after-state: what does life look like once the problem is solved? Use social proof, specific outcomes, and sensory language; make the benefit feel real and near
4. **Action — reduce friction to zero** — state exactly what to do, make it feel safe and small, and give a reason to do it now rather than later
5. **Review each section transition** — each section must flow from the previous; if the jump feels abrupt, the reader will disengage at that seam
6. **Test the attention hook in isolation** — read only the first sentence or subject line; would you continue reading if you had 40 unread emails? Revise until yes

## Rules

- The attention hook must target one specific reader archetype, not a general audience
- Interest must be built on the reader's problem, not your product's features
- Desire requires specificity: "earn $2,000 more per month" outperforms "earn more money"
- The CTA must contain a verb and communicate what happens immediately after clicking or responding
- Never place the CTA before establishing desire — it will read as presumptuous

## Examples

**Email subject (Attention):** "You're losing 2 hours a day to this one Slack habit."
**Opening (Interest):** "Every context switch costs 23 minutes of recovery time. If you're in 6 Slack channels, that's your entire afternoon."
**Body (Desire):** "Teams using async-first communication ship 40% faster and report higher focus scores. Here's the exact protocol Basecamp uses."
**CTA (Action):** "Download the 1-page async protocol — free, no email required."

## Common Mistakes

- Starting with "I" or "We": immediately signals the copy is about you, not the reader
- Skipping desire and jumping from interest to action: readers click on conviction, not curiosity alone
- Weak CTAs like "Click here": tell readers exactly what they get, not just what to do

## When NOT to Use

- Do not apply this skill to retention communications sent to existing paying customers, where leading with a problem-focused attention hook implies the customer made a poor choice and erodes relationship trust.
- Do not use this skill for regulatory or compliance communications (privacy notices, terms updates, safety disclosures) where the legal obligation is complete disclosure rather than persuasion, and the AIDA funnel would obscure required information.
- Do not apply this skill to B2B thought leadership content such as white papers or research reports, where credibility is established through depth and neutrality rather than through a persuasive sequence designed to drive immediate action.
