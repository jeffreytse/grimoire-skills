# Design Story Structure

Build a load-bearing narrative skeleton that keeps audiences emotionally engaged from page one to the final scene.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Hollywood development executives; MFA creative writing programs; game narrative designers at studios including BioWare and Naughty Dog

**Impact:** Blake Snyder's Save the Cat beat sheet is used across thousands of produced films; McKee's Story seminar has trained screenwriters whose films have earned over $2 billion at the box office

**Why best:** Audiences are wired to expect setup, escalation, and resolution. Structure is not a constraint on creativity — it is the invisible scaffolding that lets creativity land. Without it, even original ideas feel unsatisfying because they violate emotional expectations built by centuries of narrative tradition.

## Steps

1. **Identify the theme** — state in one sentence what the story is fundamentally about; every beat should test or prove this theme
2. **Define the protagonist's wound and want** — the external goal (want) drives plot; the internal need (wound) drives character; they must be in tension
3. **Place the inciting incident at 10-12%** — the event that disrupts the protagonist's ordinary world and poses the central dramatic question
4. **Design the break into act two at 25%** — the protagonist commits to pursuing the goal; no going back; the stakes become concrete
5. **Build midpoint reversal** — at the 50% mark, a false victory or false defeat that raises stakes and forces deeper commitment
6. **Write the dark night of the soul at 75%** — the protagonist's lowest point; they must choose to act from internal truth, not external pressure
7. **Land the climax and resolution** — the protagonist resolves the central conflict using skills or insight they lacked at the start; the wound is addressed

## Rules

- Every scene must advance plot, reveal character, or do both — never only one if the other is possible
- The protagonist must make the key climactic choice themselves; never resolve it through coincidence or a secondary character
- The theme must be dramatized, not stated by a character in dialogue
- Each act must end with a reversal that is surprising yet inevitable in retrospect

## Examples

**Theme:** Power corrupts those who believe they deserve it.
**Wound:** Protagonist was overlooked and undervalued.
**Want:** To become the most powerful person in the room.
**Result:** Every scene tests whether ambition can survive contact with integrity.

## Common Mistakes

- Starting with backstory instead of action: the inciting incident must arrive before the reader loses interest
- Resolving the external conflict without addressing the internal wound: the story feels hollow even if the plot resolves
- Writing a midpoint that is merely eventful rather than transformative: it must change what the story is about

## When NOT to Use

- Do not apply this skill to experimental or avant-garde literary fiction that deliberately subverts narrative convention, where imposing three-act beat positions would neutralize the formal disruption that constitutes the work's artistic statement.
- Do not use this skill for short fiction under 3,000 words, where the compressed form cannot accommodate the full structural arc and forcing beat-sheet percentages produces a rushed, mechanical feel rather than the tight unity a short story requires.
- Do not apply this skill to episodic narrative podcasts or serialized fiction where each episode must function as a standalone story, making a single overarching structural skeleton inappropriate without first designing the episode-level micro-structure separately.
