# Design Writing Practice System

Build a progressive writing practice system that develops accuracy, coherence, and register through varied tasks, targeted feedback, and iterative revision.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Cambridge English writing exam programs, Alliance Française composition courses, ACTFL writing proficiency assessment frameworks, IB language A and B programs
**Impact:** Ferris (2002) research shows focused corrective feedback on high-frequency errors reduces error rate by 40% over 10 weeks; process-writing approaches (draft → feedback → revise) produce B2-level writing 30% faster than final-draft-only methods
**Why best:** Writing externalizes interlanguage — it forces the learner to commit to grammatical choices and reveals systematic errors invisible in speech; revision cycles train self-monitoring that transfers to speaking accuracy

Sources: Ferris (2002) error treatment taxonomy; CEFR C1–C2 writing descriptors; ACTFL writing proficiency guidelines (Novice to Distinguished)

## Steps

1. **Baseline a writing sample** — prompt the learner to write 200 words on a familiar topic; analyze for error frequency by type (morphology, syntax, lexis, cohesion).
2. **Map to CEFR writing level** — apply CEFR descriptors to determine current writing level independently of speaking level; they often diverge.
3. **Define target genres** — identify the 3–5 writing genres the learner needs (email, essay, report, narrative, social post); prioritize by real-world frequency.
4. **Sequence task difficulty** — start with controlled tasks (sentence-level, fill-in-discourse-markers), progress to guided writing (structured prompts), then free writing.
5. **Implement process writing** — require draft → peer or tutor feedback → revision cycles; single-draft submission produces no error-awareness growth.
6. **Apply focused corrective feedback** — target one error type per feedback session (e.g., verb agreement, article use); unfocused correction on all errors overwhelms and has no proven advantage (Ferris 2002).
7. **Build a personal error log** — learner records each corrected error type, rule explanation, and corrected example in a running document; this log becomes the primary self-editing checklist.
8. **Assign timed writing weekly** — 20-minute timed writes simulate exam conditions and train fluency; slow deliberate writing builds accuracy but not production speed.
9. **Include register-switching tasks** — rewrite the same message as a formal email, a casual text, and a social media post; builds register awareness and lexical range.
10. **Portfolio quarterly** — collect 4–6 writing samples per quarter; compare first and latest drafts to document measurable growth.

## Rules

- Feedback must be selective, not comprehensive — marking every error in one draft is demoralizing and cognitively unprocessable.
- Revision is not optional — writing practice without revision is copying practice; the learning happens in the rewrite.
- Fluency and accuracy tasks must both be present weekly — fluency-only writing produces errors; accuracy-only writing produces slowness.
- Authentic writing tasks (real emails, real journal entries) outperform artificial prompts in motivation and transfer.

## Common Mistakes

- **Correcting every error in red pen** — comprehensive error marking is shown to produce no more improvement than focused marking and significantly more anxiety.
- **Only assigning essays** — essays are one genre; learners who only write essays cannot write professional emails, narratives, or reports accurately.
- **Skipping timed writing** — untimed-only writing produces writers who are accurate but too slow for exams and professional contexts.
- **Treating writing as separate from reading** — extensive reading is the primary source of genre and vocabulary models; writing system without reading component lacks input foundation.

## When NOT to Use

- Learner needs only oral proficiency (tour guide, customer service speaker) — writing system investment is lower priority
- Emergency functional literacy is the goal — basic phrase writing does not require a full system; target a phrasebook approach
- Learner is already at C2 writing — focus shifts to style, rhetoric, and domain-specific discourse rather than accuracy systems
