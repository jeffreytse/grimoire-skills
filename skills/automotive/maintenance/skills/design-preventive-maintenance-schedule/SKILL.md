# Design Preventive Maintenance Schedule

Build a structured, interval-based maintenance plan that keeps a vehicle reliable, safe, and cost-efficient over its lifetime.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** AAA Approved Auto Repair network (7,000+ shops), OEM manufacturers (SAE International standards), NHTSA fleet safety programs, and U.S. Department of Transportation

**Impact:** Preventive maintenance reduces breakdown risk by 75%, extends engine life by 40-60%, and lowers total repair costs by an average of $1,200/year compared to reactive repair only (AAA, 2023)

**Why best:** Interval-based scheduling aligns with component wear cycles validated through OEM durability testing. It prevents cascading failures where one neglected item degrades adjacent systems.

Sources: AAA "Your Driving Costs" 2023; SAE J1082 maintenance interval standard; NHTSA vehicle maintenance recommendations

## Steps

1. **Gather baseline data** — Collect vehicle year, make, model, trim, current mileage, and driving conditions (city/highway ratio, climate, towing use)

2. **Pull the OEM service schedule** — Locate the manufacturer's maintenance schedule in the owner's manual or OEM website; note "normal" vs. "severe" service intervals

3. **Classify driving conditions** — Determine if the vehicle qualifies for "severe" service (frequent short trips under 5 miles, extreme temperatures, dusty environments, heavy towing) and adjust intervals accordingly

4. **Map fluid service intervals** — Schedule oil/filter, transmission fluid, coolant, brake fluid, and power steering fluid changes per OEM mileage and time thresholds

5. **Schedule filter replacements** — Set intervals for engine air filter (15,000-30,000 mi), cabin air filter (12,000-15,000 mi), and fuel filter per OEM specs

6. **Plan inspection checkpoints** — At every oil change: inspect belts, hoses, brakes, tires, lights, wipers, and battery; at 30,000-mi intervals: deeper drivetrain inspection

7. **Schedule tire maintenance** — Rotation every 5,000-7,500 miles, alignment check annually or after impact, balance check with each rotation

8. **Add time-based items** — Battery test every 2 years, spark plugs per OEM (typically 30,000-100,000 mi depending on type), timing belt/chain per OEM

9. **Build a tracking log** — Create a spreadsheet or use an app (e.g., AUTOsist, Drivvo) with columns: service item, last done (date/mileage), next due (date/mileage), estimated cost

10. **Review and adjust annually** — After each major service, update the schedule; recalibrate if driving patterns change significantly

## Rules

- Always follow the stricter of time-based or mileage-based thresholds (whichever comes first)
- Use OEM-specified fluid types and grades; substitutions can void warranties and accelerate wear
- Never skip brake fluid changes — moisture absorption degrades braking performance and causes corrosion
- Keep all service receipts; records preserve resale value and support warranty claims
- For vehicles over 100,000 miles, add visual inspections of rubber components (CV boots, coolant hoses) at every oil change

## Common Mistakes

- Relying solely on the oil life monitor and ignoring other fluid and filter intervals
- Using "normal" intervals when driving conditions qualify as "severe," leading to premature component failure
- Skipping transmission fluid changes because the vehicle has "lifetime" fluid (lifetime refers to transmission life, not vehicle life)
- Neglecting coolant changes, causing internal corrosion and thermostat failure
- Overlooking cabin air filter, which reduces HVAC efficiency and air quality

## When NOT to Use

- When the vehicle is in an active breakdown or emergency situation requiring immediate roadside assistance
- When diagnosing a specific existing fault (use diagnose-check-engine-light or diagnose-brake-system-problem instead)
- When the vehicle is being sold as-is and no further maintenance investment is planned
