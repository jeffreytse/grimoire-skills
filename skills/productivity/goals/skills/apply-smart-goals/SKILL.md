# Apply SMART Goals

Write every goal as Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound to transform intentions into executable commitments.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Corporate OKR frameworks (Google, Intel), NHS clinical targets, US military unit readiness goals, university academic planning systems
**Impact:** Locke & Latham (1990): specific and challenging goals outperform "do your best" goals by up to 250% on measurable performance outcomes; Doran (1981): vague goals produce vague results — SMART criteria force the precision required for execution

**Why best:** Vague goals ("improve performance," "be healthier") fail because they provide no signal about whether you are making progress or what to do next. SMART criteria convert a wish into a decision: you either hit the target or you do not, and if not, the deviation is measurable and correctable.

## Steps

1. **Draft the goal in plain language** — Write what you want to achieve without any format constraints first
2. **Apply the Specific test** — Who, what, where, which? Replace generic nouns with named outcomes; "improve" becomes "increase by X"
3. **Apply the Measurable test** — What metric proves success? Define the unit of measurement and current baseline
4. **Apply the Achievable test** — Is the target challenging but possible given current resources and constraints? Stretch goals should have a 70% confidence of success
5. **Apply the Relevant test** — Does this goal align with broader strategic priorities? If not, deprioritise or reframe
6. **Apply the Time-bound test** — Add a specific deadline: day, month, quarter. "By end of Q2" not "soon"
7. **Schedule review checkpoints** — Set at least two interim check-ins between now and the deadline to measure leading metrics and adjust

## Rules

- If any of the five criteria cannot be satisfied, the goal is not ready to commit — rewrite or discard it
- Leading metrics (inputs you control) must be identified alongside the lagging metric (outcome); goals without leading metrics produce anxiety, not action
- Achievable does not mean easy — goals should require meaningful effort; if there is zero chance of failure, the target is too low
- Time-bound means a date on the calendar, not a relative time period unless pinned to a reference date
- Review checkpoints are mandatory; a goal with no review until the deadline is a wish with a due date

## Examples

**Vague goal:** "Get better at public speaking."

**SMART goal:** "Deliver three internal presentations of 15+ minutes to audiences of 10+ people by September 30, with a post-presentation self-rating of ≥7/10 on clarity and pacing."

**Leading metrics:** Number of practice sessions per week (input you control); **Lagging metric:** Self-rating at delivery (outcome).

## Common Mistakes

- **Measurable means trackable, not numeric** — Qualitative rubrics (rating scales, pass/fail criteria) are valid measurements; not every goal needs a number
- **Setting achievable so conservatively it requires no growth** — SMART goals should be stretch goals; comfort-zone goals waste the motivational power of specificity
- **Skipping interim checkpoints** — Reviewing only at the deadline means discovering failure too late to correct course
- **Confusing activities for goals** — "Complete 10 sales calls per week" is a leading metric; "close 3 new accounts by Q3" is the SMART goal
