# Audit Alternative Investments

Apply a structured framework to assess whether an alternative investment genuinely improves a portfolio versus adding complexity and fees without commensurate benefit.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Yale Endowment (David Swensen's model) allocates 70%+ to alternatives — the framework that drove 10%+ annualized returns for decades. The CFA curriculum dedicates an entire level to alternatives. Institutional allocators (pension funds, sovereign wealth funds) use formal evaluation frameworks before committing capital.
**Impact:** Swensen documented that alternatives added to a diversified portfolio can raise returns and reduce volatility — but only when selected rigorously and with appropriate access. Retail alternatives often feature high fees, illiquidity, and poor transparency that erode these benefits. The evaluation framework separates institutional-quality opportunities from retail wrappers designed to extract fees.
**Why best:** "Alternative investment" is a marketing category as much as an asset class. Without a structured evaluation, investors conflate genuinely uncorrelated assets (managed futures, timber) with expensive proxies for public market exposure (most liquid alts funds). The framework applies consistent criteria regardless of asset type.

## Steps

1. **Identify the investment claim** — What does the investment claim to provide? (Higher return / lower correlation / inflation hedge / income / capital preservation?) Articulate the specific portfolio problem it solves.
2. **Assess true correlation** — Does it actually behave differently from stocks and bonds in stress periods? Request or calculate correlation of returns during 2008, 2020, Q4 2018. Many "alternatives" correlate to 1.0 with equities in market crises — the moment diversification matters most.
3. **Calculate total fee load** — Alternatives are notoriously expensive. Stack all fees: management fee + performance fee (carried interest, "2 and 20" in PE/hedge) + fund-of-fund layer if applicable + redemption fees. Compare net-of-fee return expectation to a low-cost index benchmark.
4. **Evaluate liquidity terms** — Lock-up periods, redemption gates, notice periods, quarterly vs. monthly liquidity. Match liquidity to your actual need horizon. Private equity funds have 10-year lock-ups — appropriate only for capital you won't need for a decade.
5. **Apply asset-class-specific checks:**
   - **REITs**: Compare FFO yield to direct real estate cap rates; check debt levels; favor diversified over single-sector.
   - **Private equity**: Net IRR only meaningful vs. public market equivalent (PME); vintage year matters; manager selection is critical (top-quartile PE crushes median; median PE barely beats index after fees).
   - **Commodities**: Effective inflation hedge only in physical or futures-based forms; commodity company stocks have high equity correlation.
   - **Crypto**: Extreme volatility, regulatory risk, no intrinsic cash flows; sizing must reflect asymmetric downside. If including: 1–5% max; treat as venture/lottery, not diversifier.
   - **Hedge funds**: Evaluate net Sharpe ratio; survivorship bias inflates reported performance; most underperform after fees.
6. **Check minimum investment and accreditation** — Many quality alternatives require $250k+ minimums and accredited investor status ($1M net worth or $200k income). Retail access products (interval funds, BDCs) typically have worse economics.
7. **Size the allocation** — Even if the alternative meets all criteria, cap at 5–15% of portfolio. Illiquid alternatives: no more than the percentage of net worth you could lock up for 10 years without financial stress.

## Rules

- Never invest in alternatives you cannot explain the return source of — "the manager is smart" is not a return source.
- Illiquid alternatives belong only in the portion of your portfolio you genuinely won't need.
- Benchmark every alternative against a simple public market equivalent (60/40 or relevant index) net of all fees.
- Retail "alternative" mutual funds and ETFs that claim alternative strategies but trade daily are usually expensive beta in disguise — check actual rolling correlation before buying.

## Examples

**Evaluating a non-traded REIT:**
Claim: 7% yield + real estate appreciation, low correlation.
Fees: 1.5% management + 15% performance = ~2.5% total annual drag.
Liquidity: quarterly redemptions, up to 5% of NAV limit.
Correlation: 2020 COVID crash — non-traded REITs suspended redemptions (correlation irrelevant when you can't exit).
Verdict: Vanguard REIT ETF (VNQ) at 0.12% expense ratio provides similar real estate exposure with daily liquidity. Unless non-traded REIT has meaningfully superior property access, the fee differential does not justify illiquidity.

## Common Mistakes

- **Anchoring on headline yield** — High yield often means high risk, high fees, or return-of-capital disguised as income.
- **Ignoring survivorship bias** — Only successful funds remain in reported databases; reported alternative returns are systematically overstated.
- **Over-allocating to illiquid positions** — Private equity lock-ups that consume 30% of net worth can force bad decisions in liquidity crunches.
