# Design Estate Plan

Design a comprehensive estate plan that protects assets during life, ensures wishes are carried out at death, and minimizes transfer taxes.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** CFP Board (95,000+ CFPs) includes estate planning as one of six core financial planning domains; American College of Financial Services (CHFC designation) teaches estate planning comprehensively; all major estate planning attorneys and financial advisors use this framework.
**Impact:** Dying intestate (without a will) causes estate administration costs 3–5× higher than having a plan; proper trust planning can save families $50,000–$500,000+ in probate, estate taxes, and administrative costs; 55% of Americans die without a will (AARP 2023).
**Why best:** Estate planning is not just for the wealthy — everyone with assets, dependents, or specific end-of-life wishes needs a plan. Without one, state intestacy laws and probate courts make decisions on your behalf.

Sources: CFP Board Estate Planning Competency Framework; IRS Publication 950 (Estate and Gift Taxes); Uniform Probate Code; American College of Financial Services CHFC estate planning curriculum.

## Steps

1. **Inventory all assets and liabilities** — document: bank accounts, investment accounts, retirement accounts (401k, IRA), real estate (with title information), business interests, life insurance policies (face value, beneficiaries), personal property, and all debts. Note how each asset is titled and who is the named beneficiary.

2. **Identify key relationships and intentions** — determine: who are the intended heirs, in what proportions, under what conditions (outright vs. in trust). Special situations: minor children, disabled beneficiaries, blended families, estranged family members, charitable intentions, and business succession.

3. **Draft or update a Last Will and Testament** — a will is the foundation. It directs: distribution of probate assets, naming of an executor (personal representative), guardianship of minor children, and funeral/burial wishes. A will only controls probate assets — not jointly owned property, beneficiary designations, or trust assets.

4. **Evaluate the need for a Revocable Living Trust** — a revocable trust avoids probate (saves 3–7% of estate value in probate costs and 12–18 months of administration time), enables management of assets during incapacity, and provides privacy (wills are public record; trusts are private). Recommended for: estates over $150K, real estate in multiple states, complex family situations.

5. **Designate and verify all beneficiaries** — beneficiary designations on retirement accounts, life insurance, and bank/investment accounts (POD/TOD designations) pass outside probate and override the will. Review and update all designations to reflect current intentions and coordinate with the overall plan.

6. **Create powers of attorney** — Financial POA (Durable): authorizes an agent to manage financial affairs if incapacitated. Healthcare POA (Healthcare Proxy): authorizes a healthcare agent to make medical decisions. These documents are critical for planning before death — without them, court-supervised guardianship/conservatorship may be required.

7. **Draft an Advance Healthcare Directive (Living Will)** — document end-of-life care preferences: mechanical ventilation, feeding tubes, resuscitation preferences, organ donation. This relieves family members of impossible decisions during a crisis.

8. **Plan for estate and gift taxes** — Federal estate tax applies to estates over the exemption ($13.61M per person in 2024, indexed for inflation; sunsets to ~$7M in 2026 if TCJA not extended). Strategies: annual gift exclusion ($18,000 per recipient in 2024), 529 plans (front-load 5 years), irrevocable trusts (ILIT, SLAT, GRAT), charitable giving (CRT, DAF).

9. **Address business succession (if applicable)** — for business owners: document a succession plan or buy-sell agreement; ensure adequate life insurance to fund the agreement; consider a family limited partnership (FLP) or holding company for valuation discounts and transfer efficiency.

10. **Assemble a team and review regularly** — estate planning requires: estate planning attorney (drafts documents), CFP (coordinates with overall financial plan), CPA (tax implications), and insurance advisor (coverage adequacy). Review the plan every 3 years and after any major life event (marriage, divorce, birth, death, significant asset change, law change).

## Rules

- Never use online will-only services for complex estates, blended families, or significant assets — the savings are insignificant relative to the cost of errors.
- Coordinate beneficiary designations with the will and trust — conflicts between them are the #1 source of post-death family disputes.
- Store original documents securely with a copy accessible to the executor/trustee — a will in a locked safe that no one can access is useless.
- Update the plan after every major tax law change — the estate tax exemption sunset in 2026 may require significant planning revisions.

## Common Mistakes

- **Outdated beneficiary designations** — naming ex-spouses, deceased parents, or not naming contingent beneficiaries is one of the most common and costly mistakes.
- **No incapacity planning** — dying with a plan is important; being incapacitated without a plan is more immediately disruptive and expensive.
- **Funding the trust** — creating a revocable trust but not re-titling assets into it (funding) means the assets still go through probate.
- **Ignoring the step-up in basis** — gifting appreciated assets during life eliminates the step-up in cost basis at death; heirs pay capital gains tax on all appreciation. Hold appreciated assets until death to benefit from the step-up.

## When NOT to Use

- When the individual is very young with no significant assets or dependents (a simple beneficiary designation update and basic healthcare directive may suffice until circumstances change).
- When the estate plan is already recent and comprehensive and no major life events have occurred.
- When the situation involves highly complex international estate planning (multiple countries of citizenship/residency — requires specialized international estate counsel).
