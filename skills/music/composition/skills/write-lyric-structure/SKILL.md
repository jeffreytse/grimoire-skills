# Write Lyric Structure

Write song lyrics with a purposeful verse/chorus/bridge structure, consistent rhyme scheme, correct prosody matching lyric stress to melodic rhythm, and concrete imagery that makes abstract emotional content specific and memorable.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** The verse-chorus-bridge song form is the standard structure in Western popular music (pop, country, rock, R&B, hip-hop) and the foundational teaching framework at Berklee College of Music and Nashville's Belmont University songwriting programs. John Braheny's "The Craft and Business of Songwriting" and Pat Pattison's "Songwriting Without Boundaries" are the most widely used professional songwriting education texts.
**Impact:** Poorly structured lyrics bury the song's emotional core by placing the hook material in the wrong position, using imprecise abstract language that doesn't create imagery, or breaking prosody (the natural stress of words fighting against the melody). The structural and craft choices described here are not formulas — they are solutions to the problem of maximizing emotional connection and memorability within the listener's natural attention span.

## Steps

### 1. Define the song's single emotional core

Every great lyric serves one primary emotional statement:
- "Lost love from the perspective of trying to move on" (not just "a sad song about love")
- "Excitement of leaving a life behind for something new" (not "a song about change")
- "Gratitude toward a mentor who changed your trajectory" (not "an appreciation song")

Write this in one sentence before writing any lyrics. Every section of the song should serve this statement. If a verse or chorus doesn't serve it, it doesn't belong.

**The hook test:** the title (which appears in the chorus hook) should capture the song's emotional core in 1–5 words. "Hotel California," "Yesterday," "Jolene," "Born to Run" — each title immediately evokes a specific emotional space. Vague titles ("I Feel Something," "You Changed Me") do not.

### 2. Design the structure — where each section does its job

**Standard verse-chorus form:**
- **Verse:** establishes context, character, story; changes each repetition (different images, advancing narrative); lower melodic range; specific and detailed language
- **Pre-chorus (optional):** builds tension between verse and chorus; a transitional section that heightens anticipation; can use the same or different melody
- **Chorus:** the emotional peak; the point the whole song is working toward; usually the title; same lyrics repeated each time (the repetition is the point — reinforcing the central emotion); higher melodic range; broader, more universal language
- **Bridge:** provides contrast; a different perspective, register, and music; often used for emotional climax or counterargument; appears once, not repeated; gives the chorus greater weight when it returns after the bridge

**Emotional arc in the structure:** Verse 1 (setup) → Pre-chorus (tension rises) → Chorus (release) → Verse 2 (deeper or complicating context) → Pre-chorus → Chorus → Bridge (contrast/climax) → Final Chorus (resolution with new meaning from bridge)

### 3. Establish the rhyme scheme

Choose a scheme before writing and maintain it consistently:
- **AABB (couplet):** lines 1+2 rhyme; lines 3+4 rhyme; fast, driving forward momentum; common in rap and uptempo pop
- **ABAB (alternating):** lines 1+3 rhyme; lines 2+4 rhyme; more sophisticated; creates longer-form tension before resolution
- **ABCB (partial):** only lines 2+4 rhyme; more conversational feel; common in folk and country
- **Internal rhyme:** rhymes within a line, not just at the end; creates density and sophistication; common in hip-hop

**Rhyme quality:**
- **Perfect rhyme:** identical vowel and consonant sounds (day/play)
- **Near rhyme (slant rhyme):** similar but not identical (home/alone, prove/move); more sophisticated and less sing-song than forced perfect rhyme
- **Avoid forced rhyme:** distorting syntax or using unusual words to make a rhyme ("I saw you standing there, your eyes were full of flare") — the listener feels the artificiality immediately

### 4. Apply prosody — match lyric stress to melodic stress

Prosody is the alignment of the natural spoken stress of words with the melodic accent:
- Every word has a natural spoken stress pattern (TO-day, YES-ter-day, A-MA-zing, be-CAUSE)
- In the melody, certain notes fall on beats and receive rhythmic emphasis (downbeats, strong beats)
- **Correct prosody:** the word's natural stress falls on the melody's emphasized beat
- **Bad prosody:** "to-DAY" (stressed on wrong syllable to fit the melody) — listeners feel the wrongness even if they can't name it

**Prosody check:** speak the lyric in the rhythm of the melody without singing the pitch. Does it sound like natural speech? If not, the prosody is wrong. Rewrite the line until it does.

### 5. Use concrete imagery instead of abstract statements

The most common lyric weakness: abstract emotional statements instead of concrete images:
- **Weak (abstract):** "I'm so sad without you / My life feels so empty now"
- **Strong (concrete):** "Your coffee cup still on the counter / I keep forgetting to put it away"

Concrete images create the emotion; abstract statements only label it. Showing produces feeling; telling produces understanding but not feeling.

**Exercise (Pattison):** for any abstract emotional concept, list 10 concrete objects, actions, or scenes associated with that emotion. The best image from the list is usually in the first 5 or the last 5 — the safe, obvious choices are in the middle. Use the surprising but accurate image.

### 6. Write for the singer's voice — consider performance

Lyrics must be singable, not just writable:
- **Breath management:** where does the singer breathe? Marks for natural pauses should occur at phrase ends or logical breaks in the lyric, not mid-thought
- **Vowels on high notes:** vowels that are easier to sustain and project (a, ah, o, ee) are better on held high notes than closed vowels (oo, eh) or consonant-heavy syllables
- **Consonant clusters at fast tempo:** "strengths stretched" is nearly unspeakable at tempo; avoid dense consonant clusters in fast melodic passages
- **Emotional arc in the melody:** the highest note in the melody usually arrives at the lyric's most emotionally intense moment — ensure the lyric matches

## Common Mistakes

- **Same chorus every time, no variation:** a third chorus after a bridge with identical energy to the first chorus misses the opportunity. The final chorus can be bigger (higher arrangement, additional harmonies) or emotionally deepened by preceding it with a bridge that changes the frame.
- **Telling instead of showing:** "I'm heartbroken" is a statement; "I drove past your house three times" is a story. Write the scene, not the emotion label.
- **Ignoring prosody:** writing lyrics to a melody in your head without testing them against the actual rhythm of the words — a lyric that reads beautifully may sing unnaturally.

## When NOT to Use

- Avant-garde or intentionally deconstructive songwriting: experimental forms deliberately break song structure norms; the above framework applies to the mainstream-accessible tradition, not to work that derives meaning from violating those conventions.
