# Design Brand Voice Guidelines

Define a brand's verbal identity — its characteristic tone, vocabulary, personality, and communication rules — so all writers produce a consistent, recognizable voice.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Mailchimp (industry-standard Voice and Tone guide), Shopify, Atlassian, GOV.UK — all publish public voice guides as design system components
**Impact:** Consistent brand voice increases consumer trust scores by 23% (Lucidpress brand consistency research 2019); reduces copywriting revision cycles by 40% in multi-writer organizations
**Why best:** Neumeier (2003) identifies voice as a brand differentiator as powerful as visual identity; Aaker's brand personality framework provides the psychological grounding for mapping personality to vocabulary.

Sources: Neumeier "The Brand Gap" (2003) Ch. 4; Aaker "Building Strong Brands" (1996) Ch. 5; Mailchimp "Voice and Tone" (2014, public)

## Steps

1. **Audit existing communications** — collect 20–30 samples of current brand copy (ads, emails, product UI, social, support) across all channels; identify what feels on-brand vs. off-brand.
2. **Define brand personality** — use Aaker's 5 dimensions (Sincerity, Excitement, Competence, Sophistication, Ruggedness) or custom traits; select 3–4 primary traits that differentiate from competitors.
3. **Translate traits to voice** — for each personality trait, write 3–5 adjectives describing how it sounds in copy (e.g., "Competent" → "precise, direct, evidence-based, jargon-free").
4. **Define the opposite** — for each voice trait, document what the brand is NOT (e.g., "Direct but not blunt; clear but not oversimplified"); these guard rails prevent drift.
5. **Document vocabulary rules** — list preferred words, banned words, industry jargon policy, and stance on contractions, first-person plural, technical terms.
6. **Define tone spectrum** — distinguish voice (constant) from tone (contextual); document how tone shifts by context: marketing (enthusiastic), support (empathetic), legal notices (formal), error messages (calm).
7. **Create before/after examples** — for each major copy context (email subject, button label, error message, headline, social post), show a "before" (off-brand) and "after" (on-brand) rewrite.
8. **Write a reading level target** — specify target reading grade level using Flesch-Kincaid (consumer brands: 6–8th grade; B2B technical: 10–12th grade); this operationalizes "clarity."
9. **Document punctuation and formatting rules** — Oxford comma policy, capitalization style, sentence length preferences, emoji policy, exclamation mark limits.
10. **Publish and train** — embed the guide in the content design system; run a 60-min workshop with all writers; include a quiz or certification to confirm comprehension.

## Rules

- Voice must be consistent across all touchpoints — UI copy, customer support scripts, social posts, and legal notices should all feel like the same entity wrote them.
- Every voice claim must be illustrated with an example — abstract adjectives ("friendly") without examples produce inconsistent interpretation.
- Tone adaptation must be documented explicitly — writers who understand that voice is constant but tone varies produce more appropriate context-sensitive copy.
- Voice guidelines must be version-controlled — brands evolve; outdated guides create internal contradiction.
- The guide must be actionable in under 10 minutes — long guides are not consulted; prioritize the most-used rules in an accessible summary.

## Common Mistakes

- **Listing traits without examples** — "authentic, bold, human" are meaningless without sample copy that demonstrates what these mean in practice.
- **Confusing voice and tone** — "we're always friendly" is wrong if support is fielding angry complaints; tone must adapt while voice stays consistent.
- **Creating the guide without writer input** — brand strategists writing voice guides without content designers or marketers produces impractical rules.
- **No "do not" examples** — showing only correct copy leaves writers guessing where the boundaries are.
- **Never updating the guide** — a guide written at company founding may not reflect current brand position; review annually.

## When NOT to Use

- Pre-brand-definition stage (define brand strategy first)
- Single-author content operations (one consistent writer is the voice guide)
- Highly regulated industries where all copy is legally reviewed anyway (guide adds little leverage)
