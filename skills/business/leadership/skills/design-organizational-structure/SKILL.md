# Design Organizational Structure

Design an organizational structure that aligns authority, accountability, and coordination mechanisms with strategic priorities.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** McKinsey & Company, Bain, Deloitte, and enterprises undergoing growth-stage or strategic restructuring
**Impact:** Galbraith research shows misaligned org structures cause 30–50% productivity loss through coordination overhead. McKinsey data shows organizations that realign structure to strategy see 20–30% faster execution on strategic initiatives. Mintzberg demonstrated that structure misaligned to context causes systematic dysfunction regardless of individual talent.
**Why best:** Structure is not neutral — it determines information flow, decision speed, collaboration patterns, and what behaviors get rewarded. Deliberate structure design produces outcomes; default structure produces the org chart equivalent of technical debt.

Sources: Mintzberg "The Structuring of Organizations" (1979); Galbraith "Designing Organizations" (3rd ed., 2014); McKinsey "Organizational Design for Innovation" (2020)

## Steps

1. **Start with strategy, not structure** — identify the organization's primary source of competitive advantage: product innovation, operational efficiency, customer intimacy, or geographic expansion. Structure must enable the strategy; not the reverse.

2. **Audit the current state** — map the current org chart, decision rights (RACI or DACI), and coordination mechanisms. Identify structural pain points: decision bottlenecks, duplication of effort, chronic cross-team friction, unclear accountability.

3. **Select the primary structural dimension** — choose one primary axis: (a) functional (by discipline: engineering, sales, finance), (b) product/BU (by product line or business unit), (c) geographic (by region), or (d) matrix (dual reporting on two axes). The primary dimension reflects the most critical coordination requirement.

4. **Design for key cross-cutting needs** — identify the 2–3 coordination challenges that the primary structure creates. Design secondary mechanisms: shared services, communities of practice, cross-functional teams, integration roles, or overlay processes.

5. **Define spans of control** — set appropriate spans: senior leaders 5–8 direct reports, middle managers 6–10, frontline managers 8–15. Spans that are too narrow create hierarchy; spans too wide create isolation. Spans should vary by role complexity.

6. **Define decision rights explicitly** — for each major decision type, specify who decides (D), who is accountable (A), who is consulted (C), and who is informed (I). Unclear decision rights are the primary source of structural dysfunction.

7. **Size the support functions** — determine which functions are centralized (economies of scale), decentralized (proximity to business need), or shared services (both). Finance, legal, HR, and IT commonly use shared service models.

8. **Design for information flow** — map the critical information pathways required for the strategy to work. Verify the structure provides direct lines for each critical flow. Structures that require information to travel up-over-down create latency and distortion.

9. **Plan the transition** — develop a sequenced implementation plan. Restructurings that move too fast create panic and attrition. Announce the new structure with the rationale, and provide a 30–90 day transition period with clear milestones.

10. **Establish structural review cadence** — plan to revisit organizational structure annually or when strategy changes significantly. Structures that outlive the strategy they were designed for become bureaucratic obstacles.

## Rules

- Never design structure around individuals — design for the work, then staff accordingly.
- Every team must have a clear single leader with unambiguous accountability — shared leadership is a conflict-deferral mechanism.
- Matrix structures require exceptional clarity on decision rights or they become consensus traps.
- Restructuring during a crisis is often counterproductive — stabilize first, then redesign.
- Involve senior leaders in the design process but make the decision deliberately, not by committee consensus.

## Common Mistakes

- **Structure follows politics, not strategy** — designing around existing leaders' preferences rather than strategic needs produces an org chart that satisfies no one and enables nothing.
- **Excessive hierarchy** — more than 5 levels in a 500-person company signals over-management; flatten to reduce decision latency.
- **Matrix without decision rights clarity** — dual-reporting structures without explicit DACI frameworks produce gridlock.
- **Restructuring too frequently** — annual reorganizations exhaust talent and destroy institutional knowledge; restructure only when strategic alignment is genuinely broken.

## When NOT to Use

- Organizations fewer than 15 people (informal structure and direct communication are more effective)
- When the primary issue is talent, culture, or process rather than structure (structure changes will not fix people or process problems)
- Immediately after an acquisition before cultural and operational integration is complete
