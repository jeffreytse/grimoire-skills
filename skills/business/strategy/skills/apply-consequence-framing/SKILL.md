# Apply Consequence Framing

Reverse a powerful actor's decision by enumerating what it costs THEM against their own goals — not by stating your opposition.

## Why This Is Best Practice

Yan Ying (晏婴, 578–500 BC), prime minister of Qi, recorded in 晏子春秋 "内篇谏上": Duke Jing of Qi was furious that a servant had allowed a pet bird to die and ordered his execution. Yan Ying did not object. Instead, he formally charged the servant with three crimes: "He failed to preserve the bird — first crime. He caused the lord to kill a man for a bird, which will make our people believe the lord values birds over men — second crime. He will cause the lords of other states to despise our lord when they hear of it — third crime. Lord, shall we proceed with the execution?" The duke released the servant immediately.

Yan Ying's technique: reframe the decision as a cost to the decision-maker's OWN stated goals. The duke released the servant not because he was argued out of it — but because Yan Ying named the decision's downstream costs in terms the duke himself cared about.

**Adopted by:** McKinsey & Company (standard consulting practice: frame the cost of inaction/current decision before proposing alternatives — "never tell a CEO they're wrong, show them what the current decision costs on the metric they care about"); Motivational Interviewing clinical practice (Miller & Rollnick — "developing discrepancy," adopted in clinical psychology, social work, and coaching globally); FBI Crisis Negotiation (name what the current course costs the other party before requesting change); major change management practices.

**Impact:** Miller & Rollnick's Motivational Interviewing meta-analyses show significantly higher behavior change rates when clients articulate the gap between their behavior and their own values/goals, versus when external advisors argue for change directly. McKinsey consulting engagements consistently structure executive conversations as consequence revelations rather than position arguments — the approach is documented in structured problem-solving methodology taught at top consulting firms globally.

**Why best:** Direct opposition triggers ego-defensive processing. When a decision-maker hears "I think you're wrong," they shift from evaluating the decision to defending the decision. Consequence framing bypasses the defensive mechanism: the decision-maker evaluates the cost against their own goals rather than against your argument. They appear to themselves to be reconsidering based on new information, not conceding to pressure.

**Distinct from `apply-interest-alignment`:** apply-interest-alignment is pre-persuasion — you discover underlying interests BEFORE presenting a proposal, so the proposal addresses them. apply-consequence-framing is post-decision — the decision has already been announced. Your task is reversal without direct opposition.

**Distinct from `apply-reversal-probe`:** apply-reversal-probe proactively asserts the opposite of what you believe to induce the other party to reveal their true position. apply-consequence-framing addresses a KNOWN, already-announced decision — you are not probing for unknown information; you are presenting consequences of known decisions.

**Distinct from `apply-constructive-dissent`:** apply-constructive-dissent builds the structural conditions for teams to challenge decisions BEFORE commitment. apply-consequence-framing is a real-time technique for opposing a specific decision that has already been committed to, by a specific actor with more power than you.

## Steps

1. **Do not state your opposition.** The instinct when a powerful actor announces a bad decision is to say "I disagree" or "I think that's wrong." Suppress this. Stating opposition converts the conversation from decision evaluation to position defense.

2. **Identify the decision-maker's own stated goals.** What do they care about? Not what you think they should care about — what have THEY articulated as their priorities? These become the reference frame for consequence enumeration. Common goal categories: reputation, relationship, resource efficiency, long-term outcome, team morale, stakeholder reaction, legal/regulatory exposure.

3. **Map the decision's downstream costs to THEIR goals.** For each stated goal, identify what the announced decision will cost against it. Be precise: "This will cost X on metric Y" rather than "This won't be good for Z." Precision signals analysis, not opposition.

4. **Enumerate formally, in sequence.** Yan Ying enumerated three charges. The structure matters: formal enumeration signals that this is analysis, not objection. "There are three consequences worth examining. First... Second... Third..." The decision-maker follows the structure rather than mounting a defense.

5. **End with a question or silence, not a conclusion.** Do not say "...therefore we should reverse the decision." Let the consequences land. Ask: "Do you want to proceed with these consequences?" or say nothing. The decision-maker's own evaluation of the consequences against their goals produces the reversal — you do not produce it.

6. **If they proceed anyway:** Accept the decision and execute. Having enumerated the consequences, you have discharged the analytical obligation. Revisit the consequence framing when the predicted consequences materialize — not before.

7. **In high-stakes situations: prepare the consequence map before the conversation.** For significant decisions (major resource commitment, public announcement, hiring or firing), prepare a written consequence map in advance: the decision, the decision-maker's stated goals, the downstream costs on each goal, with evidence or estimates. Walk through it formally.

## Rules

- Never mix consequence framing with advocacy. The moment you say "...so I think we should do X instead," you convert consequence framing into position argument and trigger ego defense. Enumerate consequences; do not argue for alternatives.
- Consequences must be real and measurable. Vague consequences ("this could damage our reputation") are less effective than specific ones ("this will produce coverage on Y topic which typically reduces NPS by Z points based on prior incidents"). Specificity signals analysis, not opinion.
- Only enumerate consequences the decision-maker actually cares about. Enumerating consequences they don't care about wastes credibility and signals that you don't understand their priorities. Do the work to know what they care about before you begin.
- Do not use consequence framing to manipulate — enumerate genuine downstream costs, not invented ones. The technique fails immediately if a consequence proves false.

## Examples

**Strategic decision reversal:** CEO announces entering a new market before the current market is profitable. Don't say: "I don't think we should enter that market." Say: "Three things are worth examining before we proceed. First, the 24-month runway to first revenue in that market means we will hit our constraint at month 18 under current burn. Second, diverting the engineering team for 18 months will extend the current market's backlog past the competitor's expected launch date. Third, the board's current expectation is profitability before expansion — this changes that conversation. Do you want to proceed?"

**Hiring decision reversal:** Head of department announces hiring a candidate you believe will fail in the role. Don't say: "I don't think they're the right fit." Say: "Two things worth noting. Based on their prior role profile, the attrition risk in this position is high for this profile — typically 14–18 months — which costs us approximately $X in replacement. Second, the key deliverable for this role in Q3 requires [specific capability] which wasn't evident in the interview process. Do you want to proceed knowing these?"

**Regulatory/legal decision:** Founder announces a product launch that carries compliance risk you believe is unacceptable. Don't say: "I think this is too risky legally." Say: "Two regulatory exposures are worth naming. First, this launch configuration creates [specific exposure] under [specific regulation], with a $X–Y fine range if reported. Second, a competitor was sanctioned for a similar configuration in [year] — this becomes a reference case if we're investigated. Do you want to launch on this timeline with these exposures, or build in [specific time] to close them?"

**Interpersonal leadership:** Manager announces a performance decision you believe is unfair and will damage team morale. Don't say: "I think that's unfair to the employee." Say: "Three downstream effects are worth naming. The rest of the team will observe this decision and update their model of what outcomes are possible for them — current mood of the team makes this high risk for engagement. The employee has institutional relationships with two key clients who may surface concerns. And this will be the reference case in our next retention conversation with [other high performer]. Do you want to proceed?"

## Common Mistakes

- **Ending with "...so we should do X":** The conclusion converts the technique into an argument and triggers defensive response. End with a question or silence.
- **Enumerating consequences the decision-maker doesn't care about:** If they don't care about the consequence you're naming, you waste credibility and signal poor judgment. Know their actual priorities.
- **Stacking too many consequences:** Three is Yan Ying's number. More than four and the enumeration reads as opposition dressed up as analysis. Pick the three most material consequences.
- **Using the technique reactively in the moment without preparation:** Consequence framing under time pressure, without having thought through the downstream costs, produces vague consequences that don't land. For significant decisions, prepare the consequence map before the conversation.
- **Repeating the technique on the same decision after they've decided to proceed:** Once they've heard the consequences and decided to proceed, enumerate once more when the consequence materializes — not before. Repeated consequence framing before the consequence appears reads as persistence of opposition, not analysis.
