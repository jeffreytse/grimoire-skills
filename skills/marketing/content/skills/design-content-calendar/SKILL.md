# Design Content Calendar

Build a structured editorial calendar that aligns content output to business goals, audience needs, and publishing cadence.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** CoSchedule (surveyed 1,500+ marketers), Content Marketing Institute annual benchmarks
**Impact:** CoSchedule's 2024 State of Marketing report found marketers with documented editorial calendars are 3x more likely to report content marketing success

**Why best:** Ad hoc publishing produces inconsistent quality and gaps in topical coverage. A calendar enforces cadence, surfaces resource constraints in advance, and ensures content clusters are published in the right sequence (pillar before supporting posts). It also enables cross-channel coordination so social, email, and SEO content reinforce each other.

## Steps

1. **Define content pillars** — Identify 3-5 core themes that map to business objectives and audience interests; all calendar entries must map to a pillar.
2. **Audit existing content** — Catalog current published pieces by pillar, performance (traffic, conversions), and freshness; identify gaps and refresh candidates.
3. **Set publishing cadence** — Determine realistic output per channel (blog, social, email, video) based on team capacity; sustainable > ambitious.
4. **Map to business calendar** — Plot product launches, seasonal peaks, industry events, and key dates; mark these as anchor content slots.
5. **Sequence content clusters** — Publish pillar page before cluster posts; publish supporting posts within 4 weeks of the pillar to signal topical depth to Google.
6. **Assign roles per entry** — For each piece: owner, writer, editor, designer, publish date, status (planned / in-progress / scheduled / published).
7. **Build the calendar artifact** — Spreadsheet or tool (CoSchedule, Notion, Airtable) with columns: date, channel, title, pillar, keyword, owner, status, URL.
8. **Review and adjust monthly** — Hold a 30-minute calendar review; update based on performance data, capacity changes, and emerging topics.

## Rules

- Never fill a calendar without matching resource capacity — an over-committed calendar produces rushed, low-quality content.
- Every entry needs an assigned owner before it's marked "planned" — otherwise it won't ship.
- Reserve 20% of calendar slots for reactive/trending content; rigid calendars miss timely opportunities.
- Publish date and deadline are different — set deadlines 5 business days before publish to allow review.

## Examples

B2B SaaS company, Q3 calendar: Pillar "Remote Team Management" publishes July 1. Supporting posts (asynchronous tools, remote onboarding, distributed team rituals) publish weekly through August. September slot reserved for a data-driven report timed to "back to work" season. Email newsletter recaps pillar content bi-weekly. Social posts repurpose key stats from each blog post.

## Common Mistakes

- Planning without auditing existing content — recreates content already on the site, splitting SEO signals.
- Building a calendar in isolation from SEO keyword strategy — topics chosen by feel rather than demand data.
- Skipping the monthly review — stale calendars with missed deadlines lose team trust quickly.
- Treating all channels identically — LinkedIn and Instagram require different formats, not reposts of the same asset.

## When NOT to Use

- Do not build a quarterly calendar for a pre-product-market-fit startup where content strategy will pivot within weeks as the audience and offer are still being validated.
- Do not use an editorial calendar process for single one-off content pieces such as a press release or a crisis communications statement that have no ongoing publishing cadence.
- Do not apply this skill when the team lacks a dedicated content owner — a calendar without assigned accountability becomes a list of intentions, not a publishing system.
