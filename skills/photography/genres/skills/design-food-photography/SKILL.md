# Design Food Photography

Plan and execute food photography by controlling styling, angle, light, and composition to make food look appealing and authentic.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Commercial food photography is a $2B+ industry. The techniques described here are used by editorial photographers (Bon Appétit, NYT Cooking), commercial studios (product ads, menus), and content creators. Tasty's overhead format has been replicated by every major food media brand since 2015.

**Impact:** Properly styled and lit food photography increases e-commerce conversion by 30% (Adobe Commerce study, 2022) and social engagement by 2–3× compared to phone snapshots with auto settings. Restaurant menus with professional photography see 30% higher per-item revenue.

**Why best:** Food photography has unique constraints: food degrades within minutes, colors shift under different light color temperatures, and the hero ingredient must be immediately identifiable. Generic product photography rules don't transfer.

## Steps

### Step 1: Choose the angle for the story you're telling

Angle determines what is visible and what relationship the viewer has with the food.

| Angle | When to use |
|-------|-------------|
| **Overhead (90°)** | Flat dishes, pizza, grain bowls, ingredient layouts; emphasizes pattern and color |
| **45° (classic)** | Most dishes; shows depth, height, and texture; most "natural" viewing angle |
| **Eye-level (0°)** | Burgers, layered cakes, cocktails; emphasizes height and layers |
| **Three-quarter (30–60°)** | Soups, salads; compromise between flat and side view |

Default to 45° unless height or pattern are the hero.

### Step 2: Select a background and surface

Background and surface are 50% of the composition.
- **Neutral surfaces:** white marble, weathered wood, slate, linen — work with any color food
- **Warm surfaces:** terracotta, copper, warm wood — match warm-colored dishes
- **Rule:** background + dish + garnish = max 3 dominant colors (use color wheel)
- Avoid: patterned backgrounds that compete with the food; plastic-looking surfaces

### Step 3: Light with natural light or diffused artificial light

**Natural window light (recommended default):**
1. Set up 1–2 feet from a large north-facing window (indirect, even, no direct sun)
2. Place food so light comes from side or 45° behind (backlight creates appealing rim)
3. Use a white foam board opposite the window as a reflector to fill shadows
4. Avoid direct sunlight (harsh, fast-moving shadows)

**Artificial light (studio):**
1. One large softbox (60×90cm minimum) at 45° to the side and slightly behind the food
2. Small white reflector on opposite side to fill
3. Color temperature: 5500K (daylight) — do not mix with ambient tungsten light
4. Add a hair light (small snoot from above-rear) for specular highlights on liquids, oils, garnishes

### Step 4: Style the hero shot

**Hero shot** = the single image that represents the dish. It must:
1. Show the **hero ingredient** prominently — if it's a salmon dish, salmon is visible and prominent
2. Have **height and texture** — flat food looks unappetizing; add layers, garnishes, sauce drizzle
3. Show **freshness signals**: steam (add just before shooting), vibrant herbs, sauce sheen (add oil or glycerine to dull sauce), condensation on cold glass (water spray)
4. Use **odd numbers**: 3 or 5 items feel natural; 2 or 4 feel symmetrical/corporate
5. **Color contrast**: garnish of contrasting color makes the hero ingredient pop (red strawberries on cream, green herbs on tomato red)

### Step 5: Compose within the frame

1. Place the hero ingredient at a rule-of-thirds intersection — not center
2. Use **negative space** in the direction the food "faces" (see `apply-negative-space-technique`)
3. Add **props at 2/3 depth**: fork, linen napkin, ingredient, small bowl — never as prominent as food
4. Shoot a series: hero tight, hero medium pull-back with context, lifestyle (hand in frame), ingredient flat-lay
5. Leave space for text overlay if editorial use (top or left side of frame)

### Step 6: Set camera for food

- **Aperture:** f/2.8–f/5.6 for blurred background (45° angle); f/8 for overhead (everything in focus)
- **ISO:** 100–400 maximum; raise only if natural light is too dim
- **Shutter:** tripod required below 1/60s; food on a tripod = consistent framing across series
- **White balance:** set manually to light source (daylight for window, 5500K for studio) — auto WB shifts between frames in a series
- **Tether to laptop** if studio: Lightroom tethering lets you evaluate sharpness on a large screen immediately

## Rules

- Shoot the hero ingredient last — it degrades fastest; build the scene first, then add the hero
- Every frame must have a clear focal point; diffused everything = no story
- Steam, drizzle, and garnish are applied 30 seconds before the shot
- Props serve the food; food never serves the props
- Always shoot more variety than you think you need — content teams want options

## Common Mistakes

**Overhead shot for tall food:** A stacked burger from 90° is a circle of meat. Use eye-level.

**Too many props:** 5 props, 2 garnishes, a cutting board, and a herb garden fight the hero dish for attention. Remove everything until one item remains, then add back one at a time.

**Wrong white balance:** Warm tungsten ambient + cold LED main light creates color casts that are hard to remove in post. Set WB manually; use one light source.

**Forgetting freshness signals:** Plated food quickly loses its color vibrancy and moisture sheen. Keep a spray bottle and oil nearby; plate just before shooting.

**Using the same angle for every dish:** Flat dishes shot at 45° lose visual interest. Match angle to dish architecture.

## When NOT to Use

- Food **video** (Tasty-style recipe videos): different pacing and motion requirements
- **Food packaging** photography: product photography rules apply — controlled studio, exact brand color match
