# Run Landscape Photography

Plan and execute landscape shoots around light quality, weather, and foreground interest rather than arriving and reacting.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Ansel Adams' Zone System established the principle of pre-visualization — knowing what the final image will look like before pressing the shutter. Modern digital landscape photography (Joe Cornish, Charlie Waite, Michael Kenna) builds on this: the shoot is 80% planning, 20% execution.

**Impact:** Landscape photographers who arrive at a location with a planned composition, known light direction, and tide/sunrise time retrieve dramatically more usable images per outing. Reactive landscape photography — "drive somewhere nice, hope for good light" — produces tourist snapshots.

**Why best:** Landscape light peaks for 15–30 minutes per day. Missing golden hour by 20 minutes means flat midday light. Pre-planning is the only way to be in the right place at the right second with the composition already decided.

## Steps

### Step 1: Scout the location before the shoot

Never attempt a landscape cold at golden hour — you'll spend the good light finding your composition.

1. **Satellite view** (Google Maps): identify the general scene, access points, horizon obstacles
2. **PhotoPills or The Photographer's Ephemeris**: calculate exact sunrise/sunset azimuth, golden hour window, moon position
3. **Ground scout** (previous day, midday): walk the scene, test compositions, identify foreground elements, mark the exact tripod position
4. **Weather apps**: use a dedicated app (Windy, Yr.no) — cloud cover, wind, mist; overcast = soft even light; broken cloud = dramatic; rain = saturated colors post-storm

### Step 2: Choose conditions over clear skies

| Condition | Why to seek it |
|-----------|---------------|
| Golden hour (30min after sunrise / before sunset) | Warm directional light, long shadows, texture |
| Blue hour (20min before sunrise / after sunset) | Balanced sky-to-foreground exposure, deep blues |
| Storm clearing | Dramatic clouds, shafts of light, rainbow potential |
| Morning mist/fog | Atmospheric depth, layers, mystery |
| Overcast | Even light, no harsh shadows; ideal for waterfalls, forests |

Avoid midday sun unless intentional (harsh desert, abstract shadow play).

### Step 3: Set camera for landscape

Standard landscape settings:
- **Aperture:** f/8–f/11 (sharpest on most lenses; full depth of field)
- **ISO:** 100 (base ISO for cleanest file)
- **Shutter:** whatever the meter requires at f/8 ISO 100 — use tripod if below 1/30s
- **Focus:** manual or single-point AF on hyperfocal distance or 1/3 into scene
- **RAW format** (mandatory — post-processing headroom essential for landscape)
- **White balance:** daylight (not auto — consistency across bracketed frames)

### Step 4: Build foreground interest

Flat landscapes with a distant horizon fail. Strong foreground elements anchor the scene and create depth.

Good foreground elements:
- Tide pools, wet sand with reflections
- Rocks, wildflowers, fallen leaves, frost patterns
- S-curve paths, leading lines (Step 4 = `apply-leading-lines`)
- Strong shadows at golden hour

Position the camera low (tripod at minimum height) to exaggerate the foreground's scale. Get as close as f/8 allows while maintaining full depth of field.

### Step 5: Compose and bracket

1. Set composition with the foreground element at bottom-third grid line
2. Place horizon at upper or lower third (never center unless using reflection symmetry)
3. Shoot the metered exposure
4. Bracket ±1 stop for RAW blending if the sky-to-foreground contrast is beyond sensor range
5. Shoot multiple frames (5–10) at peak light — cloud shapes change second to second

### Step 6: Process for dynamic range

Landscape RAW files almost always require tonal range management:
- Pull highlights down to recover cloud detail
- Push shadows up to reveal foreground texture
- Use graduated filter (top half) to balance sky and ground
- Apply lens correction (distortion + vignette)
- Sharpen selectively for texture in foreground; keep sky clean

## Rules

- Be on location 30 minutes before golden hour starts — not at golden hour
- Use a tripod for any shot below 1/60s; no exceptions for landscapes
- f/8 at ISO 100 is the default; only deviate with a clear reason
- Shoot RAW — JPEG cannot hold the dynamic range of a landscape scene
- Always shoot with a foreground element; horizon-only compositions are weak

## Common Mistakes

**Arriving at golden hour:** Light is already peaking. Setup takes 5 minutes; light lasts 15. Arrive 30 minutes early.

**Horizon in the center:** Unless shooting a mirror reflection, a centered horizon splits the frame and says nothing. Place it at 1/3 or 2/3.

**No foreground interest:** A beautiful mountain vista 10 miles away with an empty field in front is a postcard, not a photograph. Walk forward; find rocks, flowers, water.

**Shooting in clear blue sky midday:** Flat, shadowless, high-contrast. This light is not good for landscapes. Reschedule or scout.

**Ignoring bracketing when contrast is high:** Bright sky + dark foreground exceeds sensor dynamic range. Blend 2 exposures rather than losing detail in both zones.

## When NOT to Use

- Street or environmental portraits within a landscape: `design-portrait-session` governs
- Aerial/drone landscape: different planning tools (airspace, drone regulations, perspective)
