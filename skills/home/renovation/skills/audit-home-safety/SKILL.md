# Audit Home Safety

Conduct a room-by-room home hazard assessment and produce a prioritized remediation plan.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** NFPA, CPSC, CDC, insurance underwriters, home inspectors, and elder-care organizations nationwide
**Impact:** Home fires kill 2,500 Americans/year; 36 million fall injuries occur annually; 90% of home accidents are preventable; systematic audits reduce hazard exposure by 60–80%
**Why best:** Room-by-room systematic assessment catches hazards that casual observation misses; checklists prevent cognitive overload and ensure consistent coverage

Sources: NFPA Home Safety Checklist (2023); CPSC Home Hazard Assessment Standards (2023); CDC Home and Recreational Safety (2022)

## Steps

1. **Test all smoke and CO detectors** — Press test button on every detector; replace units older than 10 years (smoke) or 5–7 years (CO). Verify placement: smoke detector on every floor and outside each sleeping area; CO detector near sleeping areas and on every floor with gas appliances.

2. **Inspect fire escape routes** — Walk every bedroom: identify two exit paths, test window operation, confirm ladder availability for upper floors. Confirm address numbers visible from street for emergency responders.

3. **Assess electrical system** — Look for: overloaded outlets, extension cords used as permanent wiring, uncovered outlets (with children present), flickering lights, warm switch plates, breaker panel with fuses or double-tapped breakers. Flag for licensed electrician.

4. **Check for fall hazards** — Inspect: stair handrails (both sides, secure), step risers (uniform height, no lips), bathroom grab bars (or absence), bath mats with non-slip backing, loose rugs, adequate lighting in stairways and hallways.

5. **Audit kitchen hazards** — Verify: fire extinguisher present and charged (Class K or ABC), appliance cords away from water, no flammable items above stove, cabinet locks if children are present, stove knob covers if applicable.

6. **Inspect water hazards** — Check: water heater temperature (set to 120°F to prevent scalding), anti-scald valves on showers, any standing water or moisture indicating leaks (mold risk), sump pump operation.

7. **Survey chemical and poison hazards** — Locate all cleaning products, medications, and pesticides; verify locked or out-of-reach storage if children or cognitively impaired adults are present. Check for unlabeled containers.

8. **Assess structural and exterior hazards** — Inspect: deck/porch structural integrity (joist connectors, post bases, railing height ≥36"), walkway trip hazards, garage door auto-reverse function, window guards (if above first floor with children).

9. **Review security systems** — Test door and window locks; check exterior lighting (motion-activated at entries); verify garage door code is not factory default.

10. **Produce prioritized remediation list** — Categorize findings: Critical (immediate life-safety risk), Major (address within 30 days), Minor (address within 6 months). Assign responsible party and estimated cost for each item.

## Rules

- Smoke detector on every level and outside each sleeping area is non-negotiable per NFPA 72.
- Water heater must be set to ≤120°F to prevent third-degree scalding in under 5 seconds.
- Never defer Critical-category findings — fire, fall, and poison hazards kill; schedule remediation same day.
- Test smoke, CO, and fire extinguisher during every audit; never assume prior audits are still valid.

## Common Mistakes

- **Only checking obvious items** — missing behind appliances (dryer lint buildup: leading cause of appliance fires), under sinks (slow leaks creating mold), and attic access areas.
- **Ignoring CO detector placement** — detectors placed too close to gas appliances generate nuisance alarms; too far from bedrooms means occupants sleep through alerts.
- **Overlooking fall hazards for adult occupants** — falls are the leading cause of injury death in adults 65+; grab bars and lighting are critical even when no elderly occupant is present (guests, future occupants).
- **Not testing extinguisher** — check pressure gauge monthly; an uncharged extinguisher in a fire is worse than none (false confidence).

## When NOT to Use

- When a professional home inspection has been completed within the last 12 months with no structural changes since
- When a certified industrial hygienist or safety inspector has conducted a formal assessment
- When the audit is for commercial or multi-family property (different codes and standards apply)
