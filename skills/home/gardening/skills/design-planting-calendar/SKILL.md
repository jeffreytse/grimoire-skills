# Design Planting Calendar

Build a personalized, zone-specific planting calendar with seed-start dates, transplant windows, and succession planting intervals.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** USDA Cooperative Extension Master Gardener programs in all 50 states; commercial market gardeners; Old Farmer's Almanac methodology used by 3M+ gardeners annually
**Impact:** Properly timed plantings increase germination success by 40%; frost-date-based scheduling prevents >70% of weather-related crop losses; succession planting extends harvest windows by 8–12 weeks
**Why best:** All timing calculations anchor to local first and last frost dates — the only reliable reference points for plant timing in temperate climates

Sources: USDA Plant Hardiness Zone Map (2023); Master Gardener Extension Programs (2023); Old Farmer's Almanac (2024)

## Steps

1. **Determine USDA hardiness zone and local frost dates** — Look up the property's USDA zone at planthardiness.ars.usda.gov. Find average last spring frost date and first fall frost date from local extension service, weather.gov, or the Almanac. These two dates anchor all calculations.

2. **Calculate indoor seed-start dates** — Count backward from last frost date using each crop's "weeks to transplant" figure:
   - Tomatoes, peppers: start 6–8 weeks before last frost
   - Brassicas (broccoli, cabbage): 4–6 weeks before last frost
   - Herbs (basil): 4–6 weeks before last frost
   - Fast crops (lettuce, radish): direct sow; no indoor start needed

3. **Calculate direct-sow dates by crop temperature tolerance:**
   - **Hardy/frost-tolerant** (spinach, kale, peas): sow 4–6 weeks before last frost (soil ≥40°F)
   - **Half-hardy** (carrots, beets, lettuce): sow 2–4 weeks before last frost (soil ≥45°F)
   - **Tender** (beans, squash, corn): sow after last frost (soil ≥60°F)
   - **Heat-loving** (melons, sweet potatoes): sow 2 weeks after last frost (soil ≥65°F)

4. **Build the fall planting window** — Count backward from first fall frost: most cool-season crops need 45–75 days to maturity. Add 2 weeks for slower fall growth (shorter days, cooling temps). This is the last planting date for fall harvest.

5. **Map the full calendar month by month** — Create a 12-month grid with crop names in the months when each action occurs: indoor start, direct sow, transplant, succession sow, harvest window, and seed saving.

6. **Design a succession planting schedule** — For continuous harvest, stagger plantings of fast crops every 2–3 weeks. Lettuce, radishes, cilantro, and bush beans benefit most from succession sowing. Stop successive plantings 60–70 days before first frost.

7. **Account for crop rotation in multi-year planning** — Divide garden into 4 rotation groups: nightshades (tomato, pepper, eggplant), brassicas, root vegetables, legumes/alliums. Rotate groups through beds in a 4-year cycle to prevent soil-borne disease buildup.

8. **Add microclimate adjustments** — Raised beds warm 2–3 weeks earlier than in-ground; north-facing slopes run 1–2 weeks later than south-facing; urban heat islands extend the season by 1–3 weeks at both ends.

9. **Incorporate perennial maintenance timing** — Add to calendar: fruit tree dormant pruning (late winter), asparagus fern cutback (fall), garlic planting (4–6 weeks before ground freeze), spring bulb planting (fall).

10. **Record actual dates and outcomes** — Track actual planting dates, germination rates, first harvest dates, and frost events. A 3-year dataset allows personal frost dates that outperform regional averages for a specific microsite.

## Rules

- All timing must anchor to local frost dates — national or regional averages can be off by 2–4 weeks for a specific site.
- Never transplant heat-lovers (tomatoes, peppers, basil) until nighttime temperatures are consistently above 50°F, regardless of frost date.
- Soil temperature is the accurate trigger for direct sowing — calendar date without soil thermometer reading leads to failed germination.
- Always harden off transplants for 7–10 days before planting out — cold-shocking plants causes stunting that reduces yield for the entire season.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using average frost dates as guarantees** — last frost date is a 50% probability; for frost-sensitive crops, use the 10% probability date (typically 2–3 weeks later).
- **Starting tomatoes too early indoors** — root-bound, leggy transplants at planting time underperform properly timed, stocky transplants.
- **Planting cool-season crops too late in spring** — lettuce and spinach sown after soil warms above 75°F bolt immediately without producing usable harvest.
- **Ignoring fall planting windows** — gardeners focused on spring miss a full second season of cool-weather crops that often outperform spring crops in pest pressure and flavor.

## When NOT to Use

- When gardening exclusively in climate-controlled indoor environments (different methodology — no frost-date dependency)
- When the planting area is in a tropical zone with no frost dates (USDA zones 11–13 use different scheduling systems)
- When purchasing established plants only with no seed starting
