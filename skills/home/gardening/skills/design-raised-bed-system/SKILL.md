# Design Raised Bed System

Design a raised bed vegetable garden by specifying bed dimensions, orientation, soil composition, irrigation layout, and a crop rotation plan to maximize yield per square foot while building long-term soil health.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Raised bed gardening is the dominant method recommended by the Royal Horticultural Society (RHS), Rodale Press, and all major vegetable gardening education programs. Eliot Coleman's "Four-Season Harvest" and John Jeavons' biointensive method — both extensively studied systems — rely on raised beds with optimized soil depth and composition. University extension programs (Cornell, UC Davis, Texas A&M) recommend raised beds as the most productive and lowest-maintenance vegetable growing approach for home gardeners.
**Impact:** Coleman (2009) reports yields 4–6× higher per square foot in optimized raised beds compared to in-ground row gardens — due to deeper, looser soil (roots penetrate more deeply), no wasted space in paths between rows, and better drainage. The no-till, deep-soil approach builds soil biology over years, reducing fertilizer and pest management inputs over time.

## Steps

### 1. Determine site and bed orientation

Site selection requirements:
- **Minimum 6 hours of direct sun daily** — more is better; leafy greens tolerate partial shade (4+ hours); fruiting vegetables (tomatoes, peppers, squash) need 8+ hours
- **Level or graded ground:** raised beds tolerate slight slopes; extreme slopes require terracing
- **Access to water:** within hose reach or plan for drip irrigation from a water source

**Bed orientation:**
- North-south orientation: preferred for most climates; exposes both sides of the bed to sun throughout the day, reducing shading of low plants by tall plants
- East-west orientation: acceptable; tall plants on the north end don't shade the bed interior

### 2. Specify bed dimensions for access and productivity

**Width:** 4 feet maximum for beds accessible from both sides; 2–2.5 feet if only accessible from one side. This allows reaching the center without stepping inside and compacting the soil — the no-compaction rule is foundational to the raised bed productivity benefit.

**Length:** 8–12 feet is practical; longer requires going around to access the opposite side; shorter is fine but less area.

**Depth:**
- **12 inches:** minimum for most vegetables; adequate for most root vegetables with loose, deep soil
- **18–24 inches:** ideal; accommodates carrots, parsnips, and creates greater water reservoir
- **Less than 8 inches:** only suitable for shallow-rooted greens (lettuce, spinach, herbs)

**Materials for the frame:**
- **Cedar or redwood:** most durable; naturally rot-resistant; 15+ year lifespan
- **Galvanized corrugated steel:** very long-lasting; modern aesthetic; no rot risk
- **Douglas fir:** less expensive than cedar; 5–7 year lifespan before rot
- **Avoid:** pressure-treated lumber with CCA (arsenic/chromium) in older formulations for food crops; modern ACQ-treated lumber is debated; cedar is the safe standard

### 3. Compose the soil mix

Standard raised bed soil mix (Mel's Mix, adapted from Bartholomew):
- **1/3 compost** (the most important ingredient; drives biology and fertility)
- **1/3 vermiculite** (improves drainage and water retention; prevents compaction)
- **1/3 peat moss or coconut coir** (water retention; structure)

Avoid filling with straight garden soil from the yard — native soil compacts in beds, drains poorly, and often contains weed seeds and pathogens.

**Amendment schedule:**
- **Initial fill:** the three-way mix
- **Annual:** top-dress with 2–3 inches of quality compost each spring; do not till (no-till preserves fungal networks); simply layer on top and let worms incorporate it

**Soil nutrition:** in year 1, a well-composed compost provides adequate initial nutrition; in subsequent years, annual compost top-dressing + occasional amendments (blood meal for nitrogen, bone meal for phosphorus) as needed based on plant performance.

### 4. Design the irrigation system

Consistent moisture is the single biggest factor in vegetable yield:
- **Drip irrigation (recommended):** emitter tubes at the root zone; 1 gallon per hour emitters spaced 6–12 inches; timed drip system ensures consistent moisture regardless of schedule; reduces foliar disease (wet leaves = fungal disease in squash, tomatoes)
- **Soaker hose:** less precise than drip but low-cost; weaves through the bed; effective for dense plantings
- **Hand watering:** viable for small gardens if done consistently; 1 inch of water per week minimum; check soil moisture 2 inches deep before watering

Install a drip timer — vegetable gardens watered on a schedule outperform hand-watered gardens in yield by 20–30% simply through consistency.

### 5. Plan crop rotation across beds

Rotating plant families between beds each season prevents soil-borne disease buildup and balances nutrient demand:

**Four-year rotation (4 beds or sections):**
- **Bed 1 (Year 1):** Nightshades (tomatoes, peppers, eggplant)
- **Bed 2 (Year 1):** Brassicas (broccoli, cabbage, kale, cauliflower)
- **Bed 3 (Year 1):** Legumes (beans, peas — add nitrogen)
- **Bed 4 (Year 1):** Root vegetables (carrots, beets, onions, garlic)

Rotate each family one bed forward each year. Nightshades should not return to the same bed for 3–4 years (prevents blight and Fusarium wilt buildup).

**Companion planting within beds:**
- Basil + tomatoes: repels aphids and whitefly; improves tomato flavor
- Nasturtiums: trap crop for aphids (aphids prefer them)
- Borage: attracts pollinators; deters tomato hornworm
- Alliums (onions, garlic): repel many insects when planted at bed edges

### 6. Manage for long-term soil health

The raised bed system improves over years as soil biology develops:
- **No-till:** never dig or rototill the bed after initial setup; disturbing the soil destroys fungal networks and weed-seed inhibition; top-dressing preserves biology
- **Cover crops (off-season):** plant winter rye, crimson clover, or buckwheat in empty beds over winter; crimson clover fixes nitrogen; all cover crops add organic matter when cut down in spring
- **Mulch paths:** 3–4 inches of wood chip mulch in paths between beds keeps weeds down and feet clean in wet weather

## Common Mistakes

- **Beds wider than 4 feet:** any width over 4 feet (accessed from both sides) forces stepping inside to reach the center, compacting soil and undermining the core raised bed benefit.
- **Filling with native soil:** compacts immediately; poor drainage; imports weeds and pathogens. Use the three-way mix.
- **Not planning irrigation before planting:** retrofitting drip irrigation into a planted bed is messy. Design and install irrigation before the first planting.

## When NOT to Use

- Large-scale food production (market gardening): raised beds are space-efficient for home use but not cost-effective at 1+ acre scales; in-ground deep tillage systems or permanent bed row systems are more practical at production scale.
