# Design Residential Lighting Scheme

Design a layered lighting scheme for a residential room by combining ambient, task, and accent layers — creating a flexible system with independent control that serves multiple uses and moods without relying on a single overhead fixture.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** The Illuminating Engineering Society of North America (IESNA) and International Association of Lighting Designers (IALD) both define the three-layer model (ambient/task/accent) as the foundational framework for residential and commercial lighting design. Philips, GE Current, and OSRAM all structure their residential lighting education around this model. Architectural lighting consultants apply layered design for all high-end residential projects.
**Impact:** Single-source overhead lighting (the standard default in most homes) flattens a room, produces harsh shadows on faces, and cannot adapt to different activities (reading vs. entertaining vs. relaxing). Research from the Lighting Research Center (Troy, NY) shows that layered lighting with separate dimmable controls is the primary determinant of a room being perceived as comfortable and inviting. Designing a lighting scheme before construction or renovation is 60–80% cheaper than retrofitting: switching, circuits, and fixture locations are determined when walls are open.

## Steps

### 1. Analyze the room's functions and light requirements

Each function requires different light levels and qualities:
- **Ambient function (movement, general use):** 30–50 foot-candles (fc); even, shadow-free illumination for navigation
- **Task functions (reading, cooking, working):** 50–100 fc at the task surface; direct, localized illumination
- **Accent/display:** 50–150 fc on the object (5:1 to 10:1 ratio above ambient); directional, focused

Map the room for: typical furniture positions, circulation paths, focal points (fireplace, artwork, architectural features).

### 2. Design the ambient layer

The ambient layer fills the room with background light:
- **Recessed downlights:** most common; place on a grid (center spacing = 1.5–2× the ceiling height from the walls); select wide-beam (90–120°) aperture for ambient; avoid pointed-down narrow beam for ambient (creates scallop shadows on walls)
- **Cove lighting:** concealed LED strips in a cove reflect off the ceiling; produces soft indirect ambient without visible sources; excellent for high ceilings
- **Pendant or chandelier:** central fixture for ambient in dining rooms or entryways; size the fixture to the room (pendant diameter in inches ≈ room diagonal in feet)
- **Wall sconces:** indirect light bouncing off the ceiling; excellent ambient quality; good for hallways and spaces with low ceilings

**All ambient lighting should be dimmable** — 0–100% range; dimming transforms the room from functional to atmospheric without changing the fixtures.

### 3. Design the task layer

Task lighting is placed close to and directed at the work surface:
- **Kitchen:** under-cabinet LED strips on the bottom shelf of upper cabinets; directed at the counter surface; 50 fc minimum at the counter
- **Desk/office:** adjustable arm lamp or pendant lowered to 12–18 inches above the desk; 50–75 fc at the surface
- **Reading chair:** floor or table lamp with the shade bottom at eye level when seated (approximately 42"–48" from floor); shade at reading height prevents glare into eyes while lighting the page
- **Vanity/bathroom:** vertical sconces at eye level on each side of the mirror (eliminates shadows under the chin and eyes that overhead-only bathroom fixtures create)
- **Bedside reading:** adjustable swing-arm wall sconce or bedside lamp at 20–30 inches above the mattress

Task lighting circuits are separate from ambient — allows task areas to be bright when working while ambient is dim for atmosphere.

### 4. Design the accent layer

Accent lighting creates visual interest by highlighting objects and architecture:
- **Artwork:** small aperture recessed adjustable fixtures (or picture lights) directed at artwork; beam spread 25–35°; position the fixture at a horizontal setback from the wall equal to 30% of the ceiling height (a 9' ceiling → fixture 2.7' from the wall)
- **Bookshelves:** small LED strip lights inside shelving or directional puck lights; illuminates objects on shelves
- **Architectural features (niches, columns, fireplace):** grazing light along the surface emphasizes texture; place the fixture close to the surface at an oblique angle
- **Plants:** an uplight at the base of a floor plant creates dramatic shadow patterns on the ceiling

Accent layer is typically low wattage; should be independently switchable from ambient.

### 5. Select light sources by color temperature

Color temperature (Kelvin) dramatically affects how a space feels:
- **2700K–3000K (warm white):** living rooms, bedrooms, dining rooms — warmth, relaxation, flattering for skin tones
- **3500K (neutral white):** kitchens, bathrooms — clean, functional, slightly cool
- **4000K–5000K (cool white/daylight):** home offices, garages, utility spaces — energizing, clinical, productive

**Rule:** all sources within one room should be within 200K of each other; mixing warm and cool in the same room creates an incoherent look.

**Color Rendering Index (CRI):** minimum 90 CRI for living spaces and anywhere skin color matters; 80 CRI acceptable for utility and exterior. CRI measures how accurately colors appear under the light source.

### 6. Plan the control system

**Dimming:** install dimmers on all circuits (ambient, task, accent independently); ensure the bulb/fixture is compatible with the dimmer (LED dimmers are different from incandescent dimmers)

**Switching zones:** each layer is a separate circuit with separate switch/dimmer:
- Ambient circuit: main dimmer
- Task circuit: independent switch
- Accent circuit: independent dimmer

**Smart control (optional):** systems like Lutron Caseta, Philips Hue, or Leviton Decora allow scene programming (combine multiple circuits at specific levels for "entertaining," "movie," "reading") — high utility for rooms used in multiple modes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Single overhead fixture for all functions:** the most common residential lighting mistake. One ceiling fixture cannot provide ambient, task, and accent simultaneously without excessive brightness or inadequate task illumination.
- **Wrong color temperature mixing:** warm pendant over the dining table + cool under-cabinet task lighting in an open kitchen = two visually disconnected zones. Coordinate color temperature across the whole space.
- **No dimming:** a room designed without dimming is locked to one brightness level regardless of time of day or activity. Dimmers are the single most impactful (and cheapest) upgrade to any lighting scheme.

## When NOT to Use

- Commercial, office, or hospitality lighting design: commercial design is governed by IESNA footcandle standards, energy codes (ASHRAE 90.1), and occupancy requirements that go beyond residential design principles.
