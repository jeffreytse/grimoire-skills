# Design Director's Shot Breakdown

Translate a scene's dramatic intent into a specific sequence of shots by planning coverage strategy, shot sizes, camera angles, and movement — communicating the plan to the DP and crew before shooting begins.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** All professional narrative film production requires a shot list and often storyboards before the shooting day. Directors from Hitchcock (famous for complete pre-planning) to Kubrick to Spielberg have described detailed pre-planning as essential to confidence on set. Sidney Lumet ("Making Movies") describes the shot list as the director's primary pre-production tool for translating intention to execution. Film schools (AFI, USC, NYU) require student directors to produce shot breakdowns before shooting.
**Impact:** An unplanned shooting day wastes time on set while the director figures out coverage — time costs money at union rates ($3,000–$10,000+ per hour for a feature set). A complete shot breakdown communicates the DP's assignment (lighting, lens selection, dolly tracks needed) and allows the AD to schedule the day efficiently. Even on low-budget productions, 1 hour of prep saves 3+ hours of improvised on-set problem-solving.

## Steps

### 1. Analyze the scene dramatically before planning shots

The shot plan serves the drama — plan the drama first:
- **What is this scene's function in the story?** (Setup, confrontation, revelation, resolution)
- **What is each character's emotional state at the start, and how does it change?**
- **What is the scene's dramatic climax — the single moment of highest tension or change?**
- **What information must the audience receive?** (Exposition, character revelation, plot point)

Mark the scene's key beats — the dramatic turning points that must be covered with a specific shot. These are non-negotiable coverage; everything else is secondary.

### 2. Define the coverage strategy

**Coverage** = the shots you capture to edit the scene in post. Three main approaches:

**Classical Hollywood coverage:**
- Master shot: wide shot of the entire scene from start to finish
- Coverage: medium shots, over-the-shoulder shots, close-ups of each character
- Pros: maximum editorial flexibility; safe against performance or pacing issues
- Cons: time-intensive to shoot; often produces middle-of-the-road results

**Designed coverage:**
- Director pre-plans the exact edit; shoots only what is needed to construct that specific cut
- Pros: efficient; highly visual results; forces directorial clarity
- Cons: less editorial flexibility; requires strong pre-visualization

**Sequence shot (long take):**
- A single, choreographed camera movement that covers the entire scene or a major portion
- Pros: continuous performance; often powerful; no cutaways needed
- Cons: requires perfect performance on every take; complex blocking and focus coordination

Choose the approach based on the scene's content, budget, and schedule. Most scenes use modified classical coverage with 3–5 designed specific shots.

### 3. Select shot sizes and angles for dramatic effect

**Shot sizes and their narrative function:**
- **Wide/establishing shot (WS/ES):** establishes geography, scale, situation; used at scene open and after major action
- **Medium shot (MS):** standard dialogue shot; shows relationship between characters
- **Over-the-shoulder (OTS):** positions viewer behind one character looking at the other; creates perspective and intimacy
- **Close-up (CU):** focuses on face or specific object; registers emotion; creates importance
- **Extreme close-up (ECU):** eyes, hands, objects of significance; maximum emphasis; used sparingly
- **Insert:** specific object detail (phone screen, gun on table, written note) — conveys information

**Angle choices and their meanings:**
- **Eye level:** neutral; naturalistic
- **Low angle (camera below eye level):** subjects appear powerful, dominant, threatening
- **High angle (camera above eye level):** subjects appear vulnerable, small, observed
- **Dutch angle (camera tilted):** psychological instability, menace, disorientation

### 4. Write the shot list

A shot list is a numbered table of every planned shot:

| Shot | Size | Angle | Movement | Description | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | WS | Eye level | Static | Ext. street — Sarah walks toward the building | Day ext.; possible drone |
| 2 | MS | Eye level | Handheld follow | Sarah enters lobby; approaches front desk | |
| 3 | CU | Eye level | Static | Sarah's face as she reads the sign | Rack focus to sign in BG |
| 4 | OTS Sarah | Eye level | Dolly-in | Receptionist tells Sarah the news | Key scene beat |
| 5 | CU | Eye level | Static | Receptionist's face — smug, unsympathetic | Reaction insert |
| 6 | ECU | Eye level | Static | Sarah's hand gripping the desk | Physical tell |

Number shots in a sequence that matches the scene's logical flow. Sequence the list to minimize lighting and camera setup changes (group all shots from one camera position before moving).

### 5. Translate to storyboard for complex scenes

A storyboard is a drawn sequence of panels showing what each shot looks like:
- Essential for complex action sequences, special effects, or stunts
- Useful for communicating with first-time collaborators or nervous financiers
- Not required for every scene — experienced directors and DPs work from shot lists alone

Storyboard panels: rough sketches showing: subject framing, dominant direction of action (arrows), approximate lighting

Production companies (Marvel, major studios): extensive pre-vis (pre-visualization, digital 3D previsualization) for all effects sequences.

### 6. Collaborate with the DP in pre-production

The shot breakdown is the basis for a prep conversation with the Director of Photography:
- Walk through the shot list together; DP identifies: lens choices, lighting implications, equipment needs (dolly? crane? Steadicam?)
- DP may suggest alternative approaches that achieve the same effect more efficiently
- Agree on color palette and visual style reference images for each scene

The shot breakdown is a starting point for collaboration, not a final unilateral directive. Great DPs contribute visual ideas that improve the plan.

## Common Mistakes

- **Shot breakdown without dramatic analysis:** planning shots before understanding the scene's emotional beats produces technically correct but emotionally empty coverage.
- **Over-shooting (too much coverage):** capturing every conceivable angle creates vast amounts of footage, slows the shoot, and makes editorial decisions harder. Design coverage, don't accumulate it.
- **Ignoring the editor's needs:** some shots only make sense in the context of the cut — plan coverage that gives the editor the specific transitions and reaction shots they need, not just beautiful individual frames.

## When NOT to Use

- Improvisational or actor-led approaches: directors like Cassavetes deliberately avoided pre-planned coverage to allow actor-driven discoveries. In this approach, the camera responds to performance rather than performance fitting camera positions.
