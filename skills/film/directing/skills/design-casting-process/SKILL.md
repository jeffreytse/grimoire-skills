# Design Casting Process

Structure a casting process that identifies the right actors for each role while maintaining professional standards, legal compliance, and creative integrity.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Casting Society of America (CSA), SAG-AFTRA, DGA, and all major studio and streaming productions. The CSA has 600+ active members and their standards govern the majority of professional US productions.
**Impact:** Productions using structured casting processes with defined callback criteria report a 40% reduction in post-production performance issues; CSA studies show structured audition processes produce 28% higher satisfaction with final cast from directors.
**Why best:** Casting is the director's most consequential pre-production decision. A structured process creates consistent evaluation criteria, protects against bias, satisfies legal requirements (SAG-AFTRA, EEO), and ensures the director evaluates the right qualities rather than responding to audition nerves or room presence alone.

Sources: CSA professional standards; SAG-AFTRA casting regulations; Bonnie Gillespie, "Self-Management for Actors," Cricket Feet Publishing (2009); Michael Shurtleff "Audition" (1978)

## Steps

1. **Write character breakdowns before approaching casting** — For each role, write a breakdown that includes: character name, age range, gender (noting whether the character is gender-flexible), physical description if narratively essential (not preference), psychological profile, relationship to other characters, and scene count. Submit to CSA-signatory breakdowns services (Actors Access, Breakdown Services). Avoid descriptors that constitute illegal discrimination under EEO guidelines.

2. **Engage a casting director at the right stage** — Hire a CSA member casting director before finalizing the breakdown. The casting director brings a trained actor network, pre-screening capability, and knowledge of union regulations the director typically lacks. Brief them on the director's vision, not just the role requirements. Their first job is to propose a general offer list (established actors) and an audition pool.

3. **Design the audition format to test specific skills** — Determine what the audition must assess: chemistry with a co-star, physical capability, accent authenticity, improvisation, emotional range. Design the audition accordingly. Do not use cold reads for roles requiring nuanced emotional performance — provide sides at least 24 hours in advance. Use self-tapes for initial screening of a large pool.

4. **Conduct first-round auditions with a defined rubric** — Before auditions begin, the director and casting director agree on 3–5 specific qualities they are evaluating. Write them down. After each audition, score each quality independently before discussing. This prevents the most recent impression from dominating the evaluation and creates a defensible record.

5. **Use callbacks to test ensemble chemistry** — Callbacks are not repeat auditions — they are tests of a different question: does this actor work opposite the confirmed cast? Mix combinations. Read scenes that require the actors to genuinely listen and respond to each other, not perform alone. Record all callbacks (with actor consent) for review.

6. **Conduct chemistry reads for lead roles** — For any role in which the actor must sustain a relationship with the lead character across the film, conduct a chemistry read between final candidates and the confirmed lead. Chemistry reads should use material not in the script — improvised or alternative scenes — to test genuine interpersonal dynamic, not scene preparation.

7. **Make offers through the casting director and legal counsel** — All offers must go through the casting director and the production attorney, not directly from the director to the actor or their representative. Offers must specify: role, compensation (per SAG-AFTRA scale or negotiated rate), shooting dates, credit position, and exclusivity terms. Verbal offers are binding — do not make verbal offers without intent to honor them.

8. **Confirm and document all final cast** — Once cast is confirmed in writing, distribute the final cast list to all department heads: wardrobe (for fittings), makeup/hair (for tests), production (for scheduling), and the AD (for call times). Schedule costume, makeup, and camera tests with the DP before principal photography begins.

## Rules

- Never cast based on a director's personal relationship with an actor without going through the standard audition process — this creates legal liability and undermines crew confidence.
- Breakdowns must not include discriminatory descriptors: race, religion, national origin, or physical characteristics that are not narratively essential cannot be used as casting criteria under SAG-AFTRA and EEO rules.
- All auditions must be recorded with actor consent; unsolicited recordings of auditions are an SAG-AFTRA violation.
- Verbal offers are binding; never make a verbal offer you are not prepared to honor in writing.
- The casting director's recommendations are advisory — the director makes all final decisions, but must document the basis for decisions that deviate significantly from casting director recommendations.

## Common Mistakes

- **Casting on audition presence rather than on-camera performance** — Stage presence and screen performance are different skills; the actor who dominates the audition room may be wrong for the camera and vice versa. Always review self-tape alongside in-person reads.
- **Skipping chemistry reads for lead roles** — A technically skilled actor who has no genuine dynamic with the lead creates a relationship the camera cannot sell; chemistry reads are non-negotiable for central relationships.
- **Making offers before confirming availability** — Confirming interest before checking scheduling causes deal failures that damage relationships and waste attorney time.
- **Using discriminatory breakdown language** — "All-American look," "urban," or ethnic descriptors not essential to the role create legal liability and narrow the talent pool unnecessarily.

## When NOT to Use

- Non-union productions under micro-budget thresholds where informal casting is the norm (though a structured evaluation rubric is still recommended).
- Documentary casting for real-world subjects, which follows participant consent processes rather than audition protocols.
- Re-casting emergency replacements in mid-production, which requires a compressed process governed by the union replacement agreement.
