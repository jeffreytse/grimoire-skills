# Apply Growth Mindset Parenting

Use research-backed language and parenting practices to build a growth mindset in school-age children — the belief that abilities develop through effort, strategies, and learning from mistakes.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** PERTS (Project for Education Research That Scales) at Stanford, used in 7,000+ schools; Microsoft, Satya Nadella's company-wide growth mindset transformation; incorporated into CASEL's SEL framework; used in National School Climate Center guidelines
**Impact:** Dweck & Blackwell (2007) longitudinal study: students taught growth mindset showed a reversal of declining math grades in 7th grade vs. control group decline; Mueller & Dweck (1998): praise for effort produced 33% improvement on subsequent challenges; praise for intelligence produced 40% performance decline after a setback; PERTS large-scale RCTs: growth mindset intervention raised GPA in lowest-performing students by 0.1 GPA points (meaningful at scale)
**Why best:** Fixed mindset (believing abilities are innate) is the primary psychological mechanism behind fear of failure, avoidance of challenge, and inability to recover from setbacks; growth mindset is the protective mechanism and is trainable through language and experience

Sources: Dweck "Mindset: The New Psychology of Success" (2006); Mueller & Dweck, JPSP (1998); Blackwell, Trzesniewski & Dweck "Implicit Theories of Intelligence" Child Development (2007)

## Steps

1. **Audit your current praise patterns** — For one week, notice what you praise: outcomes/grades/talent ("You're so smart," "You got an A!") vs. process/effort/strategy ("You kept trying that problem," "What strategy worked for you?"). Most parents default heavily to outcome praise; the audit creates awareness before change.

2. **Shift from outcome praise to process praise** — Replace "You're so talented" with "You practiced a lot and it showed." Replace "You're a natural reader" with "You've worked hard on your reading — it's paying off." The target is the process (effort, strategy, persistence) not the result or the person.

3. **Normalize mistakes as learning data** — Develop family language around mistakes: "What did you learn from that?" "That's interesting — what would you try differently?" Avoid reactions that signal mistakes are catastrophic (frustration, disappointment displays) — children read parental affect and calibrate their own risk tolerance accordingly.

4. **Use "yet" systematically** — When a child says "I can't do this," teach the habit of adding "yet": "I can't do this yet." This is not a mere linguistic trick — it activates a different attribution framework (current inability → future capability with effort) vs. fixed inability.

5. **Model your own growth mindset openly** — Narrate your struggles and learning process in front of your child: "I'm finding this recipe really hard. I burned it last time, so I'm trying a lower temperature." Children learn mindset more from observation than instruction.

6. **Challenge the fixed-mindset voice, not the child** — When a child says "I'm just not a math person," externalize the voice: "That sounds like the fixed-mindset voice. What would the growth-mindset voice say?" This gives the child agency over their own narrative rather than making the parent the advocate.

7. **Calibrate challenge appropriately** — Growth mindset flourishes in the learning zone (challenging but achievable with effort). Tasks too easy produce boredom; tasks too hard produce helplessness. Work with the child's teacher to ensure academic material is appropriately challenging.

8. **Honor the process of learning, not just the product** — Display drafts, not just final products. Celebrate the revision history of an essay, not just the grade. Show interest in what was hard, what was tried, what failed before success. This signals that the journey has value.

9. **Teach about brain plasticity explicitly** — Children as young as 6 can understand "your brain grows stronger when you practice hard things, just like a muscle." This neurobiological framing (supported by actual neuroscience) makes growth mindset concrete and memorable.

10. **Give specific, actionable feedback, not generic encouragement** — "Keep trying!" is fixed-mindset-compatible (effort without strategy). "You've been reading each word separately — try reading the whole sentence at once, then go back to the hard word" gives strategy. Effective growth-mindset feedback is specific and tactical.

## Rules

- Never praise innate traits (intelligence, talent, beauty, athletic ability) — these are fixed attributes that teach children that outcomes depend on what they were born with.
- Avoid false growth mindset: "You can do anything if you try hard enough" — growth mindset is not magical thinking; it acknowledges that effort + right strategy + good instruction produces growth, not effort alone.
- Don't dismiss genuine difficulty — "Just try harder" without acknowledging that something is genuinely hard is invalidating and counterproductive; acknowledge the difficulty, then shift to strategy.
- Apply growth mindset to social-emotional skills, not just academic ones — "I know sharing is hard right now" → "Let's figure out a strategy" is growth-mindset parenting too.

## Common Mistakes

- **Praising effort regardless of strategy** — "You tried so hard!" after ineffective effort teaches that effort alone is sufficient; pair effort acknowledgment with strategic reflection.
- **Protecting children from failure** — doing difficult tasks for them, smoothing all obstacles, intervening before struggle — removes the productive struggle that builds growth mindset; some failure is the curriculum.
- **Using growth mindset language but showing fixed-mindset reactions** — saying "mistakes are good" but visibly distressed when the child fails sends a contradictory message; the child reads the emotion, not the words.
- **Applying growth mindset only to school subjects** — limiting the framework to academics signals that it's an academic trick rather than a life philosophy; apply it across sports, friendships, creativity, and daily challenges.

## When NOT to Use

- When a child has an undiagnosed learning disability — growth mindset is necessary but not sufficient; effort applied without appropriate support for a processing disorder produces learned helplessness, not growth; ensure proper evaluation and accommodation first.
- As a response to a child in acute distress — validating the emotion comes before the growth-mindset reframe; "I know you're really upset about that grade" before "What can we learn from it?"
- To dismiss valid external barriers — growth mindset is an internal psychological orientation; it does not override real systemic disadvantages, inadequate teaching, or lack of resources; acknowledge external realities while cultivating internal resilience.
