# Design Sustainable Fashion Strategy

Build a structured approach to fashion consumption — at personal or organizational scale — that reduces environmental footprint, supports ethical supply chains, and produces a more intentional wardrobe.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Ellen MacArthur Foundation circular economy practitioners; Fashion Revolution (global movement, 70+ countries); GOTS-certified brands (8,000+ globally); B Corp-certified fashion companies; major retailers (H&M Conscious, Patagonia, Eileen Fisher)
**Impact:** Ellen MacArthur Foundation (2017) found the fashion industry produces 92 million tonnes of textile waste annually — circular strategies can reduce this by 35–50%; GOTS certification reduces chemical use by 70% compared to conventional cotton production; Fashion Revolution's transparency advocacy has driven 40%+ of surveyed brands to publish supplier lists (up from <10% in 2014)
**Why best:** Systemic strategy addresses all three impact dimensions simultaneously (environmental, social, economic) — single-dimension approaches (recycling only, or ethical sourcing only) miss 60–70% of total impact

Sources: Fashion Revolution "Fashion Transparency Index" (annual); Ellen MacArthur Foundation "A New Textiles Economy" (2017); GOTS standard documentation

## Steps

1. **Audit current consumption baseline** — track all fashion purchases for 90 days: items bought, price paid, category, and estimated CPW. Calculate annual spend and annual item count. This baseline reveals whether your impact is driven by volume (many cheap items) or value (few expensive items) — strategies differ.

2. **Identify the highest-impact reduction lever** — select the primary lever based on your baseline:
   - High-volume buyer: implement a buy-less-buy-better rule (cut item count by 50%, increase per-item quality)
   - High-spend buyer: redirect spend toward certified sustainable brands (GOTS, Fair Trade, B Corp)
   - Fast-fashion-dependent: build a 6-month shopping pause to reset purchase habits before introducing sustainable alternatives

3. **Apply the consumption hierarchy** — in order of environmental impact (best to worst):
   1. Wear what you own (most sustainable)
   2. Repair and alter existing pieces
   3. Buy secondhand (ThredUp, Poshmark, Vestiaire, local consignment)
   4. Rent or borrow (Rent the Runway, Nuuly, local clothing libraries)
   5. Buy new from certified sustainable brands
   6. Buy new from conventional brands (avoid fast fashion last)

4. **Evaluate brands using transparency criteria** — use Fashion Revolution's Transparency Index methodology:
   - Supplier list published (factory names and locations)
   - Sustainability policy public (materials, chemicals, water use)
   - Living wage commitment for supply chain workers
   - Third-party certification (GOTS, Fair Trade, Bluesign, SA8000)
   - Environmental impact reporting (Higg Index or equivalent)

5. **Select certification standards relevant to your priorities** — match certifications to your primary concern:
   - Organic fiber: GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard), OEKO-TEX STANDARD 100
   - Fair labor: Fair Trade Certified, SA8000, Better Cotton Initiative
   - Animal welfare: Responsible Wool Standard, Responsible Down Standard
   - Circular design: Cradle to Cradle Certified, Ellen MacArthur Make Fashion Circular
   - General sustainability: B Corp certification, Bluesign (chemical management)

6. **Build a secondhand sourcing strategy** — identify 3–5 platforms or local sources for secondhand purchases in your primary wardrobe categories. Set up saved searches for frequently needed items. Target: 50%+ of new wardrobe additions from secondhand sources within 12 months.

7. **Develop a garment end-of-life plan** — for each item leaving your wardrobe, designate a pathway:
   - Good condition, current style: sell (Poshmark, Vestiaire) or consign locally
   - Good condition, dated style: donate to local resale charity or clothes swap
   - Damaged but repairable: repair or repurpose (fabric scraps, cleaning rags)
   - Beyond repair: textile recycling programs (H&M Take Back, ThredUp Clean Out, local textile recyclers) — never landfill

8. **Implement a care-to-extend practice** — washing frequency, method, and temperature are the largest determinants of garment lifespan. Wear garments 2–3 times between washes unless soiled; wash at 30°C (reduces energy use 40% vs. 60°C); air dry instead of tumble drying (extends fiber life 30–50%); follow care labels; store knits folded, not hung.

9. **Calculate and track impact metrics** — annually compute: items purchased (by source: new sustainable, new conventional, secondhand, rental); average CPW for new purchases; percentage of wardrobe additions from secondhand. Set year-over-year improvement targets: 10% increase in secondhand share, 10% reduction in total item count.

10. **Engage systemic levers** — individual consumption is 30–40% of fashion's impact; policy and corporate practice drive the rest. Engage: sign Fashion Revolution petitions, support extended producer responsibility legislation, ask brands public questions (#WhoMadeMyClothes), choose brands that pay living wages and publish supplier lists.

## Rules

- The consumption hierarchy is ordered — always exhaust higher-priority options before proceeding to lower ones
- No certification substitutes for transparency — a brand claiming sustainability without a published supplier list or third-party audit cannot be verified; treat claims skeptically
- End-of-life planning must be decided at time of wardrobe audit, not deferred indefinitely — undecided items accumulate into donation bags that never leave the house
- Care practices are non-negotiable sustainability actions — they are within full individual control and have measurable impact

## Common Mistakes

- **Greenwashing susceptibility** — "eco-friendly," "sustainable," and "conscious" are unregulated marketing claims; verify with third-party certifications (GOTS, Fair Trade, B Corp) before trusting.
- **Focusing on recycling over reduction** — textile recycling recovers 10–15% of fiber value; wearing existing garments longer recovers 100% of value; reduction is 7–10x more impactful than recycling.
- **Buying new sustainable instead of secondhand** — a new organic cotton t-shirt still requires 1,500–2,700 liters of water to produce; a secondhand conventional t-shirt requires zero new resources; secondhand always beats new sustainable.
- **Ignoring labor conditions** — environmental sustainability without social sustainability is incomplete; a GOTS-certified garment made in a factory with poverty wages addresses only half of the impact equation.

## When NOT to Use

- Safety-mandated workwear procurement — compliance and safety requirements override sustainability preferences
- Emergency or disaster relief clothing contexts — urgency supersedes sustainable sourcing frameworks
- Organizational contexts where procurement policy mandates specific suppliers — advocate for policy change through appropriate channels rather than bypassing procurement rules
