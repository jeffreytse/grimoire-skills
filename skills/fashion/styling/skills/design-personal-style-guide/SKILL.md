# Design Personal Style Guide

Create a documented personal style framework — covering aesthetic identity, color palette, silhouette preferences, and shopping guardrails — that makes every future wardrobe decision faster and more consistent.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** AICI-certified image consultants (global professional standard); executive styling clients; personal brand consultants; FIT personal styling curriculum
**Impact:** AICI-trained clients report 50–70% reduction in shopping time post-style-guide; professional image consultants charge $300–2,000 for this process — documented guides eliminate recurring decision cost; Kibbe's system (used by 500k+ followers) reduces "nothing to wear" syndrome by aligning purchases to a consistent silhouette language
**Why best:** A written style guide functions as an external decision system — it removes the cognitive load of re-deciding "who I am" at every shopping or dressing moment

Sources: AICI professional standards; David Kibbe "Metamorphosis" (1987); FIT/AICI image consultant training

## Steps

1. **Define lifestyle contexts** — list all contexts requiring dress decisions: work, casual social, formal events, active/athletic, travel. Estimate weekly percentage of time in each. The style guide must serve real contexts proportionally.

2. **Conduct a visual inspiration audit** — collect 20–30 images of outfits you find compelling (Pinterest, editorial, real-world). Look for patterns: What silhouettes recur? What color families appear? What level of detail, texture, or simplicity? Name 3–5 consistent aesthetic words (e.g., "minimal," "structured," "earthy," "androgynous," "classic").

3. **Identify your Kibbe body type** — Kibbe's 13-type system classifies bodies by Yin/Yang balance (soft/curved vs. angular/straight) across bone structure, flesh, and facial features. Categories: Dramatic, Soft Dramatic, Natural, Flamboyant Natural, Classic, Soft Classic, Gamine, Soft Gamine, Flamboyant Gamine, Theatrical Romantic, Romantic. Type determines which silhouettes are inherently harmonious with your physical lines.

4. **Map type to silhouette language** — translate your Kibbe type into concrete garment shapes:
   - Dramatic: sharp angles, structured, minimal embellishment, long vertical lines
   - Classic: balanced proportions, tailored, moderate detail, symmetrical
   - Natural: relaxed structure, unconstructed, natural fabrics, layered
   - Romantic: curved seaming, soft drape, feminine detail, fitted waist
   - Gamine: contrast, cropped proportions, geometric detail, mixed elements

5. **Define personal color palette** — using PCA (personal color analysis), confirm your color season and select: 2 primary neutrals, 1 secondary neutral, 3–4 accent colors. All selected colors must appear in the inspiration images from Step 2 — this confirms alignment between color analysis and aesthetic preference.

6. **Establish a fabric vocabulary** — list 5–8 fabrics that appear repeatedly in your inspiration images and that align with your lifestyle maintenance tolerance. Include texture preferences (matte vs. sheen, structured vs. drapey, smooth vs. textured).

7. **Define style archetypes** — select 2–3 style archetypes from established frameworks (Classic, Minimalist, Romantic, Bohemian, Edgy, Preppy, Artistic, Natural) that best describe the visual language you want to project. These become your "style rules" — garments outside these archetypes require justification.

8. **Write style guidelines** — document: color palette (with specific colors named), silhouette rules (from Kibbe type), fabric preferences, 3 style archetypes, and a list of "always" and "never" items specific to your aesthetic. Example "always": structured blazers; "never": loud graphic prints.

9. **Create a shopping checklist** — before any purchase, the item must clear: (a) fits color palette, (b) fits silhouette language, (c) fits 1+ style archetype, (d) pairs with 3+ existing pieces, (e) passes CPW threshold. All 5 required.

10. **Review and update annually** — style evolves; review the guide at the annual wardrobe audit. Update aesthetic words, archetypes, or palette only if the change is supported by a new consistent pattern in inspiration images, not by a single trend influence.

## Rules

- The guide must be written and documented — verbal or mental style guides degrade into inconsistent decisions within weeks
- All five shopping checklist items are required; waiving any item must be logged as an exception with explicit justification
- Color palette and silhouette language override personal preference on any given day — the guide is a system, not a mood board
- Style archetypes may not be changed more than once per year — stability is the mechanism that builds a coherent visual identity

## Common Mistakes

- **Building the guide around aspirational identity** — selecting style archetypes and aesthetic words that reflect who you want to be, not who you currently are, produces a guide that generates guilt rather than clarity; anchor in current life, not ideal future self.
- **Selecting too many archetypes** — choosing 5+ style archetypes produces a guide with no constraints; 2–3 creates genuine decision boundaries.
- **Skipping the Kibbe silhouette step** — color and archetype work without silhouette alignment produces stylish pieces that don't harmonize on the body; all three axes (color, archetype, silhouette) must be addressed.
- **Treating the guide as permanent** — style at 25 differs from style at 40; an unreviewed 5-year-old guide becomes irrelevant and is abandoned; annual review keeps it functional.

## When NOT to Use

- Professional contexts with strict uniform requirements — the style guide becomes irrelevant when dress is mandated
- Short-term situations (hospitalization, temporary relocation) where wardrobe decisions are highly constrained
- As a first step before a wardrobe audit — audit existing wardrobe first; style guide built on an unaudited wardrobe inherits all existing dysfunction
