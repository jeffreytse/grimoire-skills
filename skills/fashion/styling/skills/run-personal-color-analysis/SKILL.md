# Run Personal Color Analysis

Identify a person's seasonal color type through systematic evaluation of skin undertone, natural hair value, eye color, and contrast level — translating findings into a practical palette of flattering colors for wardrobe and makeup use.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** The seasonal color analysis system (Summer/Winter/Spring/Autumn) was codified by Suzanne Caygill (1950s) and popularized worldwide by Carole Jackson's "Color Me Beautiful" (1980), which sold over 10 million copies and launched a global personal color consulting industry. Color analysis is a mainstream service offered by personal stylists in North America, Europe, and Japan (where it is a nationally recognized service category). Fashion houses and personal shopping services use it as the basis for client wardrobe recommendations.
**Impact:** Colors that harmonize with a person's natural coloring (skin undertone, hair value, eye color) make the face appear more alive, reduce the appearance of shadows under the eyes, and make features stand out positively. Colors that clash with natural coloring create visual fatigue, emphasize skin imperfections, and visually disconnect the wearer from their clothing. Quantifying this through analysis allows deliberate purchasing decisions rather than trial-and-error shopping.

## Steps

### 1. Assess skin undertone — the primary dimension

Skin undertone (distinct from surface color/depth) is the base hue visible beneath the skin's surface:
- **Warm undertone:** golden, peachy, yellow, or olive — veins on the inner wrist appear greenish; gold jewelry is more flattering than silver
- **Cool undertone:** pink, red, or bluish — veins appear bluish or purple; silver jewelry is more flattering than gold
- **Neutral:** neither clearly warm nor cool; both gold and silver appear flattering

**Three tests:**
1. **Vein test:** inner wrist in natural daylight — green/olive = warm; blue/purple = cool; both = neutral
2. **Jewelry test:** hold gold vs. silver near the face with no makeup; note which warms the face vs. makes it appear washed out
3. **White vs. cream test:** hold a bright white fabric then a cream/ivory fabric near the face; warm undertones look better with cream; cool undertones look better with pure white

### 2. Assess natural hair value and chroma

Hair color analysis (use natural or minimally processed color):
- **Value (light-dark):** is the natural hair light (blonde, light brown), medium, or dark (dark brown, black)?
- **Chroma (vivid vs. muted):** is the hair vibrant and saturated, or soft, ashy, and muted?
- **Undertone in hair:** warm hair has golden, red, or bronze highlights; cool hair has ash, platinum, or blue-black tones

### 3. Assess eye color and contrast level

- **Eye undertone:** brown/hazel/golden = warm; blue/grey/cool brown = cool; green is neutral-warm in most analyses
- **Contrast level:** compare hair darkness to skin lightness — high contrast (dark hair/light skin or vice versa) vs. low contrast (hair and skin values close together)

### 4. Apply the four-season model

Combine undertone, chroma, and contrast to assign a season:

| Season | Undertone | Value/Chroma | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Spring** | Warm | Light, bright, clear | Golden blonde hair; bright blue/green eyes; clear, peachy skin |
| **Autumn** | Warm | Medium-deep, muted/rich | Auburn/copper/dark brown hair; brown/hazel eyes; golden or olive skin |
| **Summer** | Cool | Light-medium, soft/muted | Ash blonde/light brown hair; soft blue/grey eyes; light, rosy/beige skin |
| **Winter** | Cool | High contrast, vivid | Dark hair + light skin, or dark hair + dark skin; vivid eye color |

Sub-seasons (16-season extension system): each season has 3–4 sub-types for finer precision, but the four-season framework is sufficient for practical wardrobe decisions.

### 5. Translate to color palette

**Spring palette:** warm, bright, light — peach, coral, warm green, warm yellow, camel, ivory, clear red, warm purple
**Autumn palette:** warm, muted, deep — burnt orange, rust, olive, mustard, terracotta, chocolate, burgundy, cream
**Summer palette:** cool, soft, muted — dusty rose, mauve, lavender, soft blue, sage, grey, burgundy, soft white
**Winter palette:** cool, vivid, high contrast — true red, royal blue, black, pure white, vivid purple, emerald, sharp navy

**Wearing colors near the face:** undertone match is most important for tops and scarves (proximity to face). Bottom half (trousers, skirts) and shoes can accommodate more neutrals from outside the season's primary palette.

### 6. Build the wardrobe anchor palette from the analysis

From the seasonal palette, select:
- **3–4 neutrals** from the season (each season has preferred neutrals — e.g., Autumn's neutrals are camel, chocolate, olive; Winter's are black, charcoal, navy)
- **2–3 accent colors** that are particularly flattering (usually from the most vivid or distinctive colors in the season)

Use this as the purchasing filter: if a garment doesn't sit within the 6–7 selected colors, reconsider before buying.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assessing in artificial light:** Color analysis requires neutral daylight, not warm incandescent or cool fluorescent light. Both distort undertone assessment significantly.
- **Using dyed/bleached hair for assessment:** Analyze natural or minimally processed hair. Heavily dyed hair creates artificial contrast that leads to incorrect season assignment.
- **Conflating skin tone depth with undertone:** A dark-skinned person can have warm OR cool undertone; a fair-skinned person can have warm OR cool undertone. Surface depth does not determine undertone.

## When NOT to Use

- Creative fashion or artistic styling contexts: deliberately breaking personal color rules is a valid artistic statement. Color analysis is a tool for ease and consistency, not a constraint for fashion exploration.
