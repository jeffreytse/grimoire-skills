# Design Outfit Formula

Design 3–5 repeatable outfit formulas for each occasion category in your life, based on proven silhouette ratios and a unified color anchor palette — eliminating daily decision fatigue while producing consistent results.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** The outfit formula concept is central to capsule wardrobe philosophy (Susie Faux, 1970s) and the "French girl wardrobe" approach that dominates contemporary minimalist style guides. Stylists for public figures (politicians, executives, celebrities) operate on formula-based dressing — Steve Jobs' turtleneck and jeans was an intentional decision-elimination formula. The concept appears in behavioral economics research on decision fatigue (Baumeister, 2011) applied to wardrobe design.
**Impact:** Sheena Iyengar's research (Columbia Business School) shows that decision fatigue from large choice sets reduces satisfaction and increases regret. Applied to wardrobes: people with large, disorganized wardrobes spend more time deciding, feel less confident in their choices, and wear 20% of their clothes 80% of the time (Pareto principle). Outfit formulas ensure the 20% is the right 20%, reduce morning decision time from 20+ minutes to 2–3 minutes, and enable deliberate purchases aligned to the system.

## Steps

### 1. Audit and categorize your lifestyle occasions

List the contexts you actually dress for (not aspirationally):
- **Work/office** (professional, casual, creative, or hybrid?)
- **Casual/errands** (weekends, social, shopping)
- **Formal/events** (weddings, dinners, black tie)
- **Active/sport** (gym, outdoor activities)
- **Home** (optional — some include, some exclude)

Calculate the realistic percentage of time in each context. Most people's wardrobe should be weighted toward their most common 2–3 contexts, not special occasions.

### 2. Select a color anchor palette

A unified color palette allows pieces to mix freely:
- **Neutral anchors (3–4 colors):** build the majority of wardrobe around these; everything in this group works with everything else. Common anchors: navy, black, grey, camel, ivory, white, chocolate brown.
- **Accent colors (1–2 colors):** add personality; should complement your neutrals. Limit to 1–2 to maintain mix-and-match flexibility.

Test palette cohesion: lay all your current clothes on a bed; can every top work with every bottom? If not, identify which items break the system — those are candidates for replacement or donation.

**Personal color undertone (optional but useful):** warm undertones (golden/peachy skin) are often flattered by warm neutrals (camel, ivory, olive); cool undertones (pink/blue-veined skin) by cool neutrals (charcoal, navy, true white).

### 3. Design silhouette formulas per occasion category

A silhouette formula defines the proportions for an outfit regardless of specific garments:

**Core silhouette formulas:**
- **1/3-2/3 ratio:** one volume piece + one fitted piece; e.g., oversized top + slim trousers, or slim top + wide-leg trouser
- **Tucked formula:** any top partially or fully tucked defines the waist and separates the outfit into proportioned zones
- **Layering formula:** base + layer (jacket, blazer, coat) of consistent length relative to trousers
- **Tone-on-tone:** same or similar color in different textures; monochromatic; inherently elongating

Assign 1–2 formulas per occasion category. The formula is the template; individual garments are interchangeable within the formula.

### 4. Build formulas with specific garments

For each formula, identify which specific garments execute it:
- List anchor pieces that appear in the most formulas (e.g., a good-fit blazer, a well-cut trouser, a quality t-shirt)
- These are "capsule foundation" items — worth highest quality investment
- Items that only work in one formula are "specialty" items — may not justify the space in a smaller wardrobe

Create a simple matrix: rows = garments, columns = formulas. Any garment appearing in 3+ formulas is high-utility; anything in only 1 formula is a candidate for removal.

### 5. Test formulas in practice

Before committing to the system:
- Try each formula for 1 week; document what you reach for and what you don't
- Photograph successful outfits with your phone (a style journal) — builds a reference library
- Note gaps: is there a formula you want but lack pieces to execute? These are the only justified new purchases

The test phase reveals real vs. theoretical versatility — what looks good on paper doesn't always feel right in practice.

### 6. Apply formula thinking to new purchases

Before buying: "which existing formula does this piece belong to, and does it replace or extend an existing garment?" If no clear answer → don't buy it.

Capsule wardrobe size targets:
- **Minimalist:** 30–40 total pieces including shoes and outerwear
- **Standard capsule:** 50–65 pieces
- **Lifestyle-adjusted:** scale up for professions requiring formality range (lawyers, politicians) or creative fields with higher visible variety expectation

## Common Mistakes

- **Building formulas with aspirational items:** Formulas built around items worn only at ideal weight, perfect occasion, or imagined future lifestyle don't work daily. Build from what you wear now.
- **Too many accent colors:** Each additional accent color reduces the number of valid combinations. Two accent colors halve the mixing possibilities; three quarter it.
- **No review cycle:** Formulas become outdated as lifestyle changes. Review annually — change in job, life stage, or body changes the occasion mix and what pieces are needed.

## When NOT to Use

- Personal style exploration phase: someone still discovering what they like should explore freely before systematizing. Formulas are useful for people who know what works; they constrain discovery for those still searching.
