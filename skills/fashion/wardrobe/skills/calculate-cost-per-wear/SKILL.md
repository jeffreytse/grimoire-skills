# Calculate Cost Per Wear

Determine the real economic value of a garment by dividing total ownership cost by projected wear count, enabling rational comparison between cheap-but-disposable and expensive-but-durable options.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Personal finance advisors; sustainable fashion educators; Project 333 participants; Fashion Revolution transparency advocates
**Impact:** Wrap UK (2012) found extending garment life by 9 months reduces carbon, water, and waste footprints by 20–30%; CPW analysis shifts consumer preference from fast fashion (avg CPW $3–8) to quality investment pieces (CPW $0.50–1.50); Fashion Revolution data shows price does not correlate with quality above a certain threshold
**Why best:** CPW converts emotional purchase impulses into comparable financial units — the same mental framework used for any capital expenditure

Sources: Fashion Revolution "True Cost" analysis; Courtney Carver Project 333; Wrap UK "Valuing Our Clothes" (2012)

## Steps

1. **Define the formula** — Cost Per Wear (CPW) = Total Ownership Cost ÷ Projected Wear Count. Target CPW: ≤$1.00 for everyday items, ≤$2.00 for occasion wear, ≤$5.00 for once-per-year formal items.

2. **Calculate total ownership cost** — include: purchase price + alterations/tailoring + dry cleaning (annual cost × expected years owned) + repairs. Exclude sunk costs on items already owned.

3. **Estimate projected wear count** — multiply: expected wears per week × 52 × years you'll own it. Use conservative estimates: fast fashion lifespan 1–2 years, quality basics 5–10 years, investment pieces 10–20 years.

4. **Apply lifestyle reality check** — before projecting wear count, verify: Does this fit your actual lifestyle? Does it fit your current body now (not aspirational)? Does it pair with 3+ pieces you already own?

5. **Compute CPW and benchmark** — divide total cost by projected wears. Compare against your CPW ceiling. Example: $200 coat, worn 3×/week for 8 months/year for 10 years = 240 wears/year × 10 = 2,400 wears; CPW = $0.08 — excellent.

6. **Compare alternatives** — run CPW on each option being considered. A $30 fast-fashion equivalent lasting 1 year at 1×/week = 52 wears; CPW = $0.58 — still acceptable, but factor in quality degradation after wash cycle 20.

7. **Factor in resale value** — for quality items, subtract estimated resale value from total ownership cost. Designer and quality basics retain 30–60% resale value on platforms like ThredUp, Poshmark, or Vestiaire.

8. **Apply the regret test** — if CPW is borderline acceptable, ask: "Will I regret not buying this in 30 days?" If uncertain, wait 48 hours; impulse passes, genuine need remains.

9. **Document the decision** — log purchase decisions (buy/pass) with CPW rationale. Review quarterly to calibrate projection accuracy — did you actually wear it as often as projected?

10. **Recalculate on audit** — at each wardrobe audit, retrospectively calculate actual CPW for items being retired. Compare projected vs. actual to improve future estimates.

## Rules

- Never calculate CPW on an item that fails the 3-outfit combination test — no wear count projection is credible if the item has no outfit partners
- Resale value may only be subtracted if you have a concrete plan to sell (not "someday I'll sell it")
- CPW ≤$1.00 is the threshold for everyday items; above this, the item needs strong justification
- Dry cleaning costs must be included — ignoring maintenance costs understates true CPW by 15–40% for delicate fabrics

## Common Mistakes

- **Overestimating wear frequency** — projecting 5×/week for an item you'll realistically wear 1×/week inflates the wear count and falsely validates the purchase; use current actual behavior, not aspirational.
- **Ignoring maintenance costs** — a $150 silk blouse requiring $8/cleaning, cleaned 12×/year for 5 years, adds $480 in maintenance — nearly tripling the effective cost.
- **Applying CPW to justify emotional purchases** — CPW is a filter, not a rationalization tool; if you're running CPW numbers after deciding to buy, you're using it backwards.
- **Forgetting opportunity cost** — the $200 spent on a low-CPW item is $200 not available for a higher-CPW alternative; always compare the best alternative, not an abstract ceiling.

## When NOT to Use

- Safety or professional compliance items (PPE, required uniforms) — CPW is irrelevant when purchase is mandatory
- Rental or borrowing scenarios — CPW applies to ownership decisions only
- Gifts — the recipient's lifestyle and preferences, not CPW, should drive gift selection
