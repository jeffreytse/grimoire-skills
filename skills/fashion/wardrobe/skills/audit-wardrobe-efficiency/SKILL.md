# Audit Wardrobe Efficiency

Systematically evaluate every garment owned to surface the true utilization rate and eliminate pieces that consume space without delivering outfit value.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** KonMari-certified consultants (3,000+ globally); Project 333 participants; sustainable fashion educators; professional stylists
**Impact:** KonMari practitioners report keeping 60–80% fewer items post-audit; Fletcher (2008) found the average garment is worn only 7 times before disposal — auditing triples that rate; users report 20–40 minutes saved daily in getting-dressed decisions
**Why best:** Objective tracking replaces emotional attachment as the decision criterion — utilization data, not guilt or nostalgia, drives retention decisions

Sources: Marie Kondo "The Life-Changing Magic of Tidying Up" (2011); Project 333 (Courtney Carver); Kate Fletcher "Sustainable Fashion and Textiles" (2008)

## Steps

1. **Block dedicated time** — schedule 3–4 uninterrupted hours; do not audit in increments, as incomplete audits reset emotional momentum and produce inconsistent decisions.

2. **Extract everything** — remove all clothing from closets, drawers, under-bed storage, and off-site storage into one room. Hidden items are systematically underused.

3. **Sort into categories** — group by type: tops, bottoms, dresses/jumpsuits, outerwear, formalwear, shoes, accessories. Do not sort by color or season yet.

4. **Apply the utilization test** — for each piece, answer: "Have I worn this in the past 12 months?" Yes → keep pile. No → question pile. Worn but damaged/ill-fitting → repair-or-retire pile.

5. **Run the combination test on keep pile** — for each kept piece, name 3 other kept pieces it pairs with to form a complete outfit. Pieces that cannot form 3 outfits move to the question pile.

6. **Evaluate question pile by category** — sentimental items: photograph and donate; aspirational items (fit, lifestyle): retire unless the life change is concrete and scheduled within 90 days; genuine gaps: note the need but do not fill during the audit.

7. **Assess condition** — inspect kept pieces for pilling, fading, loose threads, broken closures. Grade: A (excellent), B (good), C (needs repair), D (retire). Commit to repair dates for C items or retire them.

8. **Calculate utilization rate** — count total pieces owned before audit, count pieces retained, compute retention rate. A healthy capsule wardrobe retains 30–50% of a typical pre-audit wardrobe.

9. **Identify functional gaps** — map kept pieces against lifestyle ratios (work, casual, formal, active). Note categories with fewer than 3 complete outfit combinations as real gaps for future deliberate shopping.

10. **Document and store** — photograph the final kept collection, organized by category. Store the image as a reference before shopping trips to prevent redundant purchases.

## Rules

- All categories must be audited in one session — partial audits preserve the worst decisions
- Utilization data (12-month wear history) overrides emotional attachment in retention decisions
- Do not shop during or immediately after an audit — gaps become clear only after the full collection is visible
- Every piece that stays must have a named outfit it completes

## Common Mistakes

- **Auditing by category across multiple days** — emotional state changes between sessions, producing inconsistent keep/retire decisions; complete all categories in one sitting.
- **Keeping "just in case" items** — items retained for rare hypothetical events (a black-tie event you haven't attended in 3 years) are dead weight; rent or borrow for genuine rare occasions.
- **Skipping shoes and accessories** — accessories and shoes consume 20–30% of wardrobe space and budget but are routinely excluded from audits, leaving the largest sources of redundancy untouched.
- **Retiring without replacement planning** — discarding pieces without noting genuine gaps leads to panic-buying poor replacements; document gaps before donating.

## When NOT to Use

- Within 6 months of a major life change (new job, relocation, significant weight change) — wait for the new context to stabilize before auditing
- When emotionally depleted — audits made under stress produce regrettable decisions; reschedule
- As a substitute for addressing compulsive shopping — audit reduces stock but does not address the acquisition behavior
