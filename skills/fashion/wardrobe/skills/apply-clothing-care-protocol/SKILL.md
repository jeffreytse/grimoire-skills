# Apply Clothing Care Protocol

Extend garment lifespan by applying fiber-specific washing temperatures, drying methods, pressing techniques, and storage practices — maintaining fabric performance and appearance while reducing replacement frequency.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** International care label standards (ISO 3758, ASTM D5489) define the care symbols on all garment labels in the US, EU, and internationally. Woolmark (The Wool Development International) publishes fiber-specific care standards used by all major retailers. The Ellen MacArthur Foundation's Fashion and Circular Economy report (2017) identifies extended garment lifespan as one of the most impactful changes available to consumers — a garment worn 2× as long produces half the environmental impact per use.
**Impact:** Research from WRAP (UK) shows the average garment in the UK is worn 40 times before disposal; increasing wear to 80 times reduces its lifetime environmental impact by 43%. Incorrect washing is the primary cause of premature garment failure — shrinkage (hot water on wool/cotton), color fading (hot water or tumble drying on dyed fabrics), and fiber breakdown (over-agitation) are all care protocol failures. Quality garments treated correctly last 5–15 years; the same garments washed incorrectly last 2–3 years.

## Steps

### 1. Read and interpret care labels before washing anything

Care symbols (ISO 3758) are the authoritative source — always check first.

**Washing tub symbols:**
- Number inside tub = max water temperature (°C)
- Hand icon = hand wash only
- X through tub = do not wash (dry clean only)

**Common temperatures:**
- 30°C (86°F): cool, for delicates and colors
- 40°C (104°F): warm, for most cottons and synthetics
- 60°C (140°F): hot, for white cottons and highly soiled items

**Drying symbols:**
- Square = tumble dry (dots = heat level: 1 dot = low, 2 = medium, 3 = high)
- Square with horizontal line = dry flat
- Square with curved line = line dry
- X through dryer = do not tumble dry

### 2. Apply fiber-specific washing rules

**Wool:**
- 30°C maximum; use wool-specific detergent (neutral pH; no enzymes)
- Gentle/hand wash cycle; minimal agitation (agitation felts wool fibers permanently)
- Do not wring or twist — press excess water out gently
- Dry flat (hanging stretches the wet fiber)

**Cotton (unbleached, white, or pale):**
- 40–60°C depending on soil level
- Standard cycle acceptable; 60°C for towels and bed linen
- Tumble dry low-medium; remove slightly damp to prevent over-drying

**Cotton (dyed or dark):**
- 30–40°C; cold wash preserves color
- Turn inside-out; reduces surface friction and color loss
- Dry out of direct sunlight (UV fades dyes)

**Silk:**
- 30°C hand wash or machine on silk/delicate cycle
- Silk-specific detergent (pH neutral; no alkali which degrades silk proteins)
- Never tumble dry; dry flat in shade
- Iron on silk setting (low heat, slightly damp, with pressing cloth)

**Linen:**
- 40–60°C; linen improves with washing
- Tumble dry acceptable (adds softness); remove before completely dry
- Iron damp on hot setting — linen is difficult to press when fully dry

**Synthetics (polyester, nylon, acrylic):**
- 30–40°C; synthetics hold dye well at cool temperatures
- Low-heat tumble dry (high heat can deform synthetic fibers)
- Do not use fabric softener on moisture-wicking activewear (softener blocks the wicking function)

**Cashmere:**
- 30°C hand wash; same as wool protocols
- Baby shampoo as a substitute detergent (pH neutral, gentle)
- Dry flat; reshape while damp

### 3. Apply the right pressing technique per fabric

Pressing removes wrinkles and restores shape; ironing creates them (ironing moves the iron; pressing sets it in place):

**General pressing rules:**
- Always press on the wrong side (inside) of the fabric to prevent shine
- Use a damp pressing cloth between iron and fabric for delicates
- Iron temperature by fiber (check fabric content):
  - Synthetic: low heat (1 dot)
  - Wool/silk: medium (2 dots) with steam; use pressing cloth
  - Cotton/linen: high heat (3 dots) with steam

**Press direction:** follow the grain of the fabric; pressing against the grain distorts shape.

### 4. Store correctly per fiber type

**Hanging:** for structured garments (jackets, blazers, dresses with weight), use wide shouldered hangers that support the shoulder shape; wire hangers stretch shoulders
**Folding:** for knitwear (wool, cashmere, heavy cotton); hanging stretches knit fabric over time
**Drawer storage:** roll instead of fold for t-shirts and casual items; reduces wrinkle and allows seeing all items
**Cedar blocks or lavender sachets:** natural moth deterrent for wool and cashmere storage; replace moth balls (naphthalene) which leave chemical odors

**Before seasonal storage:**
- Wash everything before storage — body oils, food residues, and skin particles attract moths and accelerate fabric degradation
- Store clean garments in breathable fabric garment bags (not sealed plastic, which traps moisture)

### 5. Treat stains immediately

The longer a stain sits, the harder to remove:
- **Protein stains (blood, milk, egg):** cold water immediately — never hot (heat sets protein); enzyme detergent
- **Oil/grease stains:** blot (don't rub); apply dish soap or dry cleaning solvent; wash at appropriate temperature
- **Tannin stains (coffee, tea, wine):** cold water flush; white wine on red wine; enzyme cleaner
- **Dye transfer:** cold water; color-safe bleach if fabric allows; rewash before drying (heat sets dye transfer permanently)

## Common Mistakes

- **Tumble-drying everything on high:** High heat in the dryer damages elastic, shrinks cotton, deforms synthetic fibers, and weakens seams. Use the lowest effective heat.
- **Washing everything at 60°C for "hygiene":** 60°C fades colors, shrinks fibers, and breaks down fabrics significantly faster than necessary. Most cottons and synthetics clean perfectly at 30–40°C.
- **Storing unwashed garments:** Proteins and oils from body contact attract moths and accelerate fiber degradation during storage. Always wash before putting away for the season.

## When NOT to Use

- Specialty textiles (museum-quality textiles, heavily beaded or embroidered garments, antique fabrics): these require textile conservation expertise, not standard care protocols — professional conservation or dry cleaning specialists should be consulted.
