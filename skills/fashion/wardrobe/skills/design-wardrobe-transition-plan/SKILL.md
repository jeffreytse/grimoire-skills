# Design Wardrobe Transition Plan

Design a seasonal wardrobe transition by systematically storing off-season pieces, assessing what survived the previous season, identifying gaps, and preparing the incoming wardrobe — maintaining a functional, gap-free closet year-round.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Seasonal wardrobe transition is central to capsule wardrobe methodology (Anuschka Rees "The Curated Closet") and Kondo's KonMari tidying system. Professional personal stylists include seasonal wardrobe audits as a standard service — assessing condition, identifying gaps, and planning targeted purchases. The WRAP (Waste and Resources Action Programme) study found that 30% of clothing has not been worn in the last year — seasonal transitions are the systematic opportunity to address this.
**Impact:** An unmanaged wardrobe grows through accumulation without strategic removal — each season adds pieces without retiring others, producing crowding, lost items, and reduced functionality. A systematic transition takes 2–3 hours twice per year and produces: cleaner storage, better visibility into what you own, a specific gap list for intentional purchasing (rather than impulse buying), and extension of garment life through proper seasonal storage.

## Steps

### 1. Time the transition to actual weather, not the calendar

Seasonal transition timing:
- **Spring → Summer (put away winter pieces):** when a week of 60°F+ (15°C+) average is consistent; not just one warm day
- **Autumn → Winter (put away summer pieces):** when a week of 55°F (13°C) average is consistent; before you need the winter pieces in an emergency

Transitional pieces (denim jackets, medium-weight sweaters, layering items) should remain accessible during the transition period — don't store them yet.

### 2. Complete the outgoing season review first

Before putting away or storing anything, assess each piece from the outgoing season:
**For each garment — answer honestly:**
1. Did I wear it this season?
2. Does it still fit and flatter?
3. Is it in good condition?
4. Does it belong to my current style?

**Outcomes:**
- Worn + fits + good condition + current style → **store for next season**
- Worn but damaged, faded, or stretched → **decide: repair or donate/discard**
- Not worn despite opportunity → **honest assessment: why? Wrong fit, wrong style, duplicate, or truly needed?** If truly needed, store with a note; if not — donate
- Never worn (still tagged or untouched) → **donate; you were not wrong to buy it, but continuing to store it is a sunk cost**

The outgoing season review is the most honest reflection of wardrobe function — what you reached for tells you more than what you intended to wear.

### 3. Clean and prepare pieces before storage

Clothing should never be stored dirty:
- Wash or dry clean every piece before seasonal storage
- Repair any small issues now (loose buttons, broken zippers, small tears) — coming back to a damaged piece next season requires the same effort plus forgetting why it was stored
- Check for moth damage on wool and cashmere: small holes, or seeing the actual moth or larvae; treat immediately with cedar blocks or bay leaves; consider professional dry cleaning for heavy infestation

### 4. Store properly — protect from moths, moisture, and compression

**Storage containers:**
- Canvas or natural fiber storage bags (breathable): best for most garments
- Sealed plastic bins: only for non-fiber items or areas with pest risk (add cedar or lavender blocks)
- Never sealed vacuum bags for knits/wools long-term — compresses and deforms fibers

**Storage methods:**
- Knits: folded flat, not hung (hanger marks and stretching)
- Heavy coats: hang on padded hangers in a breathable garment bag
- Delicate items: wrapped in acid-free tissue paper
- Natural fibers (wool, cashmere): cedar blocks, dried lavender, or silica gel sachets added to storage

Label all storage clearly with contents and season.

### 5. Set up the incoming season wardrobe and identify gaps

As you bring the incoming season wardrobe forward:
1. **Condition check:** re-assess each incoming piece as you unpack it (condition degrades in storage)
2. **Fit check:** try on anything you're unsure about — bodies change; don't assume fit
3. **Color/style check:** does this still fit your current style and color palette?

**Gap identification:**
After setting up the incoming wardrobe, wear it for 1–2 weeks before buying anything. The gaps — "I have nothing for this" or "I've reached for this 5 times and it doesn't exist" — become your only justified shopping list.

Write the gap list: be specific (not "I need a skirt"; instead "I need a midi-length navy blue skirt for work"). Specificity prevents impulse substitutes that don't actually fill the gap.

### 6. Set a shopping budget and timeline for genuine gaps

Gap-based shopping vs. trend-based shopping:
- Gap-based shopping: you know exactly what you need, why, and what specifications it must meet
- Trend-based shopping: reactive to what's new, often producing items that don't fill any gap

For each identified gap:
- Define: color, fit, fabric, occasion purpose
- Set price ceiling based on use frequency (frequent = invest more; rare = spend less)
- Set timeline (don't rush — the right piece is better than the fast piece)

## Common Mistakes

- **Storing dirty clothes:** Dirt and body oils set during storage, become impossible to remove, attract moths, and degrade fibers. Always clean before storage.
- **No gap review — shopping while emotional:** buying outside the gap list during the seasonal transition produces new pieces that duplicate what you have or don't fit the system, perpetuating the accumulation problem.
- **Storing aspirational pieces instead of honest removal:** a piece you haven't worn in 2 full seasons is not a future outfit candidate — it is occupying storage and mental space. Donate.

## When NOT to Use

- Year-round single-climate wardrobes (e.g., tropical climates with no seasonal shift): if temperature variation is minimal, a full seasonal transition is unnecessary; apply the assessment and gap-identification steps quarterly instead.
