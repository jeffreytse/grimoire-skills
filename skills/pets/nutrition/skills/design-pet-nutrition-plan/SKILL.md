# Design Pet Nutrition Plan

Build an evidence-based feeding plan covering food selection, portion sizing, meal frequency, and diet monitoring.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** WSAVA (World Small Animal Veterinary Association), AAHA, American College of Veterinary Nutrition, veterinary nutritionists globally

**Impact:** Proper nutrition prevents or manages obesity (affecting 55–60% of US dogs and cats), reduces risk of diet-related urinary disease (up to 50% reduction with appropriate moisture content for cats), and extends healthy lifespan by 1.8 years in dogs maintained at ideal body condition (Kealy et al., 2002)

**Why best:** AAFCO nutritional adequacy statements and WSAVA guidelines provide the only evidence-based framework for evaluating commercial pet food; without these, owners rely on marketing claims unconnected to nutritional science.

Sources: AAFCO Official Publication (2024); WSAVA Global Nutrition Guidelines (2011); NRC Nutrient Requirements of Dogs and Cats (2006); Kealy et al., "Effects of diet restriction on life span and age-related changes in dogs" JAVMA (2002)

## Steps

1. **Assess the patient** — record species, breed, age, reproductive status (intact/spayed/neutered), body condition score (BCS, 1–9 scale), activity level, and any current health conditions.

2. **Confirm life stage** — select food formulated for the correct life stage: growth/puppy-kitten, adult maintenance, all life stages, or senior; verify the AAFCO nutritional adequacy statement on the label.

3. **Evaluate current diet** — review existing food for AAFCO compliance, ingredient quality, manufacturer's feeding trial history, and known recalls.

4. **Apply WSAVA food selection criteria** — prioritize foods from companies that: employ a full-time board-certified veterinary nutritionist, conduct feeding trials, publish research, and have quality control manufacturing.

5. **Calculate daily caloric requirement** — use resting energy requirement (RER = 70 × body weight in kg^0.75) multiplied by a life stage factor (1.6 for neutered adult dogs; 1.2–1.4 for adult cats); adjust for activity.

6. **Determine portion size** — divide daily caloric target by the food's caloric density (kcal/cup or kcal/can from manufacturer); split into 2 meals/day for adults, 3–4 for puppies/kittens.

7. **Plan treat budget** — treats should not exceed 10% of daily caloric intake; account for training treats in the daily total.

8. **Address water intake** — for cats, incorporate wet food (≥70% moisture) to support urinary health; ensure fresh water is available at all times; consider a water fountain for cats reluctant to drink.

9. **Schedule body condition monitoring** — weigh monthly and reassess BCS; adjust portions by 5–10% increments based on BCS trend, not owner perception.

10. **Document and communicate the plan** — write feeding instructions including food name, portion per meal, meal frequency, treat limit, and next weight-check date.

## Rules

- Never feed a diet that lacks an AAFCO nutritional adequacy statement for the correct life stage.
- Any diet change must be transitioned over 7–10 days to prevent gastrointestinal upset.
- Homemade diets require formulation by a board-certified veterinary nutritionist (diplomate ACVN); generic recipes are nutritionally incomplete.
- Weight loss programs for obese pets must be veterinarian-supervised; rapid weight loss in cats risks hepatic lipidosis.

## Common Mistakes

- **Feeding by volume rather than calories** — different foods have widely varying caloric densities; volume-based feeding causes consistent over- or under-feeding.
- **Relying solely on ingredient lists** — the quality and bioavailability of ingredients matter more than whether "chicken" appears before "chicken meal."
- **Ignoring treats in caloric accounting** — training-heavy periods with no treat adjustment routinely produce 15–20% caloric excess.
- **Switching foods abruptly** — causes diarrhea and vomiting; owners attribute to food quality rather than transition speed.

## When NOT to Use

- When the pet has an active disease requiring therapeutic nutrition (renal disease, pancreatitis, diabetes) — defer to a veterinarian or veterinary nutritionist for prescription diet selection.
- When the pet is at a healthy weight with no behavioral or health concerns and is eating a complete, balanced, AAFCO-compliant diet — no intervention needed.
- As a substitute for a physical examination — body condition scoring requires hands-on palpation by a trained professional for accurate classification.
