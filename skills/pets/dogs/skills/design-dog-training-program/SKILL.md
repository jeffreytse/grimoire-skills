# Design Dog Training Program

Build a structured, LIMA-compliant training program tailored to a dog's age, breed, and behavioral baseline.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** APDT (Association of Professional Dog Trainers), IAABC (International Association of Animal Behavior Consultants), veterinary behaviorists worldwide

**Impact:** Force-free, LIMA-based programs show 70–90% success rates for common obedience goals with <5% relapse when maintenance is followed; aversive methods linked to 2–3× increased aggression risk (Herron et al., 2009)

**Why best:** Least Intrusive, Minimally Aversive (LIMA) approach selects the lowest-impact intervention that achieves the goal, preserving the dog-owner bond while avoiding learned helplessness and fear side-effects.

Sources: APDT LIMA Guidelines (2012); Herron et al., "Survey of the use and outcome of confrontational and non-confrontational training methods" JAVMA 2009; Karen Pryor "Don't Shoot the Dog" (1984)

## Steps

1. **Collect baseline profile** — document age, breed, sex/neuter status, daily schedule, prior training history, and known triggers.

2. **Identify training goals** — list specific behaviors to establish (sit, recall, leash manners) and behaviors to reduce (jumping, barking), ranked by safety priority.

3. **Run a brief behavioral assessment** — observe the dog for 10–15 minutes across contexts (greet, play, leash, handling) to note reactivity, arousal thresholds, and motivators.

4. **Select primary reinforcers** — determine highest-value rewards (food type, toy, praise) through a preference test; rank for use in ascending-difficulty tasks.

5. **Draft a LIMA hierarchy plan** — for each target behavior, map the least intrusive method first (antecedent management, positive reinforcement) before considering negative punishment or negative reinforcement.

6. **Structure session schedule** — plan 2–3 sessions per day, 5–10 minutes each for puppies; 10–15 minutes for adult dogs; include rest and generalization days.

7. **Define measurable milestones** — set criteria (e.g., "sits on cue 8/10 trials in 3 different locations") before moving to the next difficulty level.

8. **Plan generalization** — schedule practice across locations, people, and distractions using a systematic distraction gradient.

9. **Build in a maintenance protocol** — specify weekly practice requirements and monthly check-ins after the primary program concludes.

10. **Review and adjust at each milestone** — reassess if progress stalls for more than two consecutive sessions; consult a certified professional if aggression or fear is present.

## Rules

- Always start with the least intrusive, most positive method before escalating.
- Never use pain, fear, or intimidation as a training tool.
- Pair every new environment or stimulus with positive experiences before asking for behaviors.
- Keep sessions short enough to end while the dog is still succeeding.

## Common Mistakes

- **Skipping the baseline assessment** — leads to generic programs that miss breed- or individual-specific needs, reducing effectiveness.
- **Progressing too fast** — jumping difficulty levels before criteria are met causes frustration and can erode the behavior.
- **Inconsistent reinforcement schedules** — variable owner follow-through creates extinction bursts and regression.
- **Training only in one location** — behaviors fail to generalize; dogs learn "sit in kitchen," not "sit."

## When NOT to Use

- When the dog displays overt aggression toward people or other animals — refer to a veterinary behaviorist first.
- When medical causes (pain, thyroid, neurological) for behavior have not been ruled out.
- When the owner cannot commit to daily practice — the program will fail without implementation.
