# Apply Pre-Performance Routine

Design and execute a structured sequence of physical, cognitive, and behavioral actions that reliably activate optimal performance state before competition.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** AASP sport psychologists, Olympic sports programs (gymnastics, diving, archery, golf), professional team sports for set-pieces and free throws
**Impact:** Athletes using consistent pre-performance routines show 15-25% improvement in skill execution under pressure vs. no-routine control groups (Lidor & Mayan, 2005); NBA free-throw accuracy is 5-8% higher with consistent pre-shot routines
**Why best:** Routines reduce cognitive interference by redirecting attention from outcome anxiety to process cues; they trigger learned associations between specific behaviors and optimal arousal states (classical conditioning mechanism)

Sources: AASP position papers; Lidor & Mayan Sport Psychologist (2005); Weinberg & Gould (2019)

## Steps

1. **Identify optimal performance state** — With the athlete, describe what "feeling ready to compete" looks and feels like: arousal level (1-10), focus quality, body sensations, and self-talk tone.

2. **Audit current pre-performance behavior** — Document what the athlete currently does in the 30-90 minutes before performance; identify helpful and unhelpful existing habits.

3. **Design physical warm-up sequence** — Build a consistent physical activation sequence (dynamic stretch, movement prep, sport-specific drills) that anchors the body to competition readiness.

4. **Select attentional cues** — Choose 2-3 process-focus cue words (e.g., "smooth," "attack," "present") that redirect attention from outcome anxiety to controllable execution elements.

5. **Add a mental rehearsal segment** — Include 3-5 minutes of visualization: athlete vividly imagines executing key skills with confidence, using all sensory modalities (sight, feel, sound).

6. **Establish a time structure** — Map routine to a consistent timeline (e.g., 90 min: arrive and change; 60 min: team warm-up; 20 min: individual prep; 5 min: cue activation).

7. **Practice the routine in training** — Rehearse the full routine before practice sessions and simulated competitions; the routine must be automatized before high-stakes events.

8. **Create arousal regulation tools** — For under-aroused athletes: energizing music, high-intensity movement bursts. For over-aroused athletes: box breathing (4-4-4-4), progressive muscle relaxation.

9. **Test and refine** — After each competition, rate whether the routine achieved the target state (1-10); adjust elements that consistently fail to produce readiness.

10. **Build in contingencies** — Prepare a shortened version (5-10 min) for situations where time is disrupted (delays, schedule changes); the core anchor behaviors must still occur.

## Rules

- The routine must be athlete-driven, not coach-imposed; athletes who own their routine execute it more reliably under stress
- Never introduce routine changes immediately before a major competition; refine only in training or low-stakes events
- Routine length should match competition context (individual sport: 15-30 min; team sport: 5-10 min individual phase within team warm-up)
- Physical and mental components are both required; purely physical or purely mental routines are less effective than combined approaches

## Common Mistakes

- **Routine too long or complex** — routines requiring 20+ steps cause cognitive overload and break down under pressure; 5-8 key behaviors is optimal.
- **Never practicing the routine** — athletes who design a routine but only use it in competition have no automaticity; it adds stress instead of reducing it.
- **Copying another athlete's routine** — what activates one athlete may de-activate another; individualization is essential.
- **Ignoring arousal direction** — routines designed to calm an under-aroused athlete make performance worse; always match routine to the athlete's arousal problem.

## When NOT to Use

- Team tactical briefings — pre-game team meetings serve a different purpose and should not substitute for individual preparation
- Injury warm-up protocols where medical rehabilitation requirements override routine design
- Athletes who perform better with spontaneous, less structured preparation — forced routines can increase anxiety in this profile
