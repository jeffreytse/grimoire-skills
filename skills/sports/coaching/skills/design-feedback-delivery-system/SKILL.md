# Design Feedback Delivery System

Create a structured, consistent approach to delivering performance feedback that accelerates skill acquisition and builds athlete self-correction capability.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** USGA teaching programs, PGA Teaching Manual, USTA coaching certification, and UEFA coaching education all include feedback methodology as core curriculum. John Wooden's coaching philosophy — which produced 10 NCAA championships at UCLA — centered on specific, immediate, corrective feedback delivered at high frequency with minimal praise or criticism.
**Impact:** Hattie & Timperley (2007) meta-analysis of 7,000+ studies found feedback has one of the highest effect sizes on learning (d=0.79), but only when it is specific, timely, and actionable. Magill (2014) synthesis of motor learning research shows that augmented feedback (external feedback from a coach) accelerates skill acquisition in early learning, but over-reliance retards self-regulatory skill development. The optimal feedback frequency decreases as skill level increases.

## Steps

### 1. Define the feedback goal by athlete level

Adjust feedback approach by learning stage:
- **Beginner:** high frequency, prescriptive ("foot placement here, arm angle here"), immediate
- **Intermediate:** reduced frequency (every 2-3 attempts), questioning mixed with prescription
- **Advanced:** delayed (post-set or post-drill), athlete self-evaluates first before coach adds

The goal of coaching feedback is ultimately to develop the athlete's self-correction ability. Coaches who give feedback on every rep create dependency, not skill.

### 2. Keep feedback specific and behavioral

Effective feedback names a specific observable behavior, not a general quality:
- **Ineffective:** "Your passing was off today."
- **Effective:** "Your plant foot is pointing at the defender rather than the target — redirect it toward the target."

Use the WHAT-WHY-HOW structure:
1. WHAT: name the specific behavior ("Your elbow dropped in the third set of pull-ups.")
2. WHY: explain the consequence ("This reduces lat engagement and loads the bicep instead.")
3. HOW: give the correction cue ("Lead with your chest, keep elbows tracking behind you.")

### 3. Set the positive-to-corrective ratio

Research consensus (Gottman's work translated to coaching; Gallup coaching literature):
- **Minimum:** 3:1 (positive-to-corrective) to maintain athlete confidence and receptiveness
- **Optimal:** 5:1 in early learning; decreases to 2:1 as athlete advances
- **Practical:** count your feedback interactions for one session to calibrate your natural ratio

Positive feedback must be specific behavior, not general praise ("Good job" → "Your weight transfer was perfect there — same technique.").

### 4. Time the feedback correctly

Timing guidelines from motor learning research:
- **During the action:** only for safety issues or gross errors — interrupting execution disrupts motor patterns
- **Immediately after:** best for beginners and major technical errors
- **After a set/series:** better for intermediate; allows the athlete to develop internal feedback first
- **Post-session:** for tactical or strategic feedback that requires context

The KR delay (knowledge of results delay): a 3-5 second pause after performance before giving feedback improves retention vs. immediate feedback (Swinnen et al., 1990).

### 5. Use questioning to develop self-correction

Before giving a correction, ask:
- "How did that feel to you?"
- "What do you think you'd change?"
- "Where did you lose control of [X]?"

If the athlete can self-identify the error: reinforce their observation and confirm the correction. If they can't: guide with a question ("What was your elbow doing?") before providing the answer.

### 6. Build a feedback-safe environment

Feedback culture is as important as feedback content:
- Correct errors without emotional charge — neutral tone for corrections, genuine for praise
- Distinguish performance feedback from character evaluation: "Your timing was off" ≠ "You're sloppy"
- After a correction, watch for successful execution and acknowledge it immediately

## Common Mistakes

- **Correcting everything:** Coaches who correct every error create anxiety and reduce automaticity. Select the 1-2 most important errors per session.
- **Vague praise that inflates ego without building skill:** "You're so talented" disconnects success from controllable effort. Name the behavior.
- **Only correcting errors:** Athletes need to know what they're doing right to repeat and build on it.

## When NOT to Use

- Immediately before a competition — feedback that introduces doubt or technical change before a match is harmful. Rely on learned patterns.
- During an emotional outburst or injury — address the athlete's emotional/physical state first.
