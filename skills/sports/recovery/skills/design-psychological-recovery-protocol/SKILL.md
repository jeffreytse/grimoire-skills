# Design Psychological Recovery Protocol

Structure a deliberate mental recovery process after performance setbacks, competitive losses, or periods of burnout — to process the experience constructively, restore confidence, and prevent identity entanglement with results.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** USOC Mental Performance Program, Australian Institute of Sport mental skills program, and national sport psychology associations include post-competition psychological recovery as a core competency for high-performance athletes. The All Blacks, New Zealand national rugby team, use formalized post-game "flush" rituals and psychologist-supported debrief processes as part of their culture.
**Impact:** Gould et al. (1997) study of junior tennis burnout found that athletes who lacked effective emotional recovery strategies showed significantly higher burnout incidence. Research in sport psychology (Beilock & Carr, 2001; Rotella and colleagues) shows that athletes who ruminate on mistakes during subsequent performance show significantly impaired performance — the failure to psychologically close a setback directly creates the next underperformance. A structured recovery process closes the psychological loop.

## Steps

### 1. Allow the initial emotional response (don't suppress)

Immediately after a setback:
- Suppress nothing — frustration, disappointment, and anger are appropriate responses to caring about performance
- Establish a "flush window" — a defined period (30-60 min post-competition) where athletes are allowed to feel and express the emotions without analysis or coaching
- Do not force an immediate positive reframe; premature positivity is dismissive of the emotional experience

The team bus, dressing room, or first 24 hours is not the moment for deep tactical debrief — it is for emotional processing.

### 2. Conduct a structured performance review (24-48 hours later)

Once the emotional intensity has reduced:
- Review performance objectively: what was executed well? What was the primary contributor to the outcome?
- Separate controllable factors (effort, execution, game plan adherence) from uncontrollable factors (opponent quality, officiating, weather)
- Identify 1-3 specific, actionable takeaways that would change the outcome in a future identical situation

The format: no more than 30-60 minutes; written responses if the athlete responds better to reflection than verbal discussion.

### 3. Close the loop — make a next-step commitment

After the review, the athlete must shift from analysis to forward planning:
- Identify one specific training action that addresses the primary controllable factor identified
- Set a date and time for that action
- This transforms the setback from a source of rumination into a catalyst for specific preparation

The psychological shift from "what went wrong" to "what I will do about it" is the closing mechanism.

### 4. Address identity entanglement

Common in elite athletes: self-worth tied to performance → a bad performance feels like a referendum on who they are.
- Help the athlete distinguish performance from identity: "You had a bad game. You are not a bad athlete."
- Explore: what do you value about yourself that exists independently of results?
- For coaches: be consistent in your behavior toward athletes regardless of performance — treating athletes differently after losses communicates that their value is performance-dependent

If an athlete shows persistent signs of identity entanglement (withdrawal, depression, inability to train after setbacks), refer to a sport psychologist.

### 5. Build pre-competition psychological closure rituals

For repeated competition (weekly games, tournaments): athletes cannot fully process each setback before the next competition arrives. Build a ritual that closes the previous event psychologically:
- "One play rule" (Bill Walsh): once a mistake is recognized and understood, the athlete allows themselves one replay, then it is closed permanently
- Pre-game journal entry: "What I learned last time; what I'm committed to today"
- Physical transition ritual: arrive at the next training with a deliberate reset behavior (specific music, a particular warm-up move, a word repeated)

### 6. Recognize and address burnout

If an athlete shows persistent symptoms beyond normal post-loss negativity:
- Chronic emotional exhaustion despite normal sleep and training load
- Loss of intrinsic motivation for training (the sport feels like obligation, not choice)
- Depersonalization: going through the motions without engagement
- Cynicism about performance, teammates, or the value of competition

These are burnout indicators (Maslach's three-factor model applied to sport). Intervention: reduce training load, increase autonomy, restore fun and social connection — and involve a clinical professional.

## Common Mistakes

- **Immediate post-loss tactical debrief:** Coaches who deliver tactical feedback within 30 minutes of a loss are talking to athletes who cannot process technical information — emotional circuits override analytical processing.
- **Forcing positivity:** "Shake it off" dismisses the athlete's genuine experience and prevents real processing. Allow the negative feeling; guide the next step.
- **No closure mechanism:** Athletes who never psychologically close setbacks accumulate anxiety that progressively degrades performance.

## When NOT to Use

- Active mental health crisis (anxiety disorder, depression, trauma) — requires clinical professional, not a coaching protocol.
