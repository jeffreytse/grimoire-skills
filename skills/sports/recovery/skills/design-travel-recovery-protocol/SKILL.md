# Design Travel Recovery Protocol

Plan pre-travel circadian adjustment, in-transit recovery strategies, and post-arrival routines to minimize travel-related performance impairment for athletes competing away.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** NBA teams, international cricket boards (ICC travel protocols), British & Irish Lions tours, and Olympic teams traveling to distant host cities (Tokyo, Beijing) all use structured travel recovery protocols developed with sleep scientists and sports medicine teams. UK Sport provided all Team GB Olympic athletes with detailed travel recovery plans before Tokyo 2021.
**Impact:** Fowler et al. (2015) meta-analysis found travel across ≥4 time zones impairs peak power output, reaction time, and mood for 1-4 days depending on direction of travel and individual response. Jet lag eastward travel is consistently more disruptive than westward travel. Without intervention, performance impairment peaks at 1-2 days post-arrival. With a structured protocol, athletes can accelerate adaptation by 1-2 days — often the difference between competing impaired or at full capacity.

## Steps

### 1. Assess the travel demand

Determine the key variables before designing the protocol:
- **Time zone difference:** ≤3 zones = minor adjustment; 4-8 zones = significant; >8 zones = severe
- **Direction:** eastward (earlier sleep required) = harder; westward (later sleep) = easier
- **Competition timing relative to arrival:** same day? 1 day? 3+ days?
- **Duration:** short trip (<3 days) — stay on home time zone; longer — adapt to local time

### 2. Pre-travel circadian adjustment (3-5 days before departure)

For significant time zone differences (≥4 hours):
- **Eastward travel:** advance sleep schedule 30-60 min/day for 3-4 days before departure; use morning bright light exposure to advance the clock
- **Westward travel:** delay sleep schedule 30-60 min/day; use evening bright light at destination to delay the clock
- Take meals and exercise at target local time 1-2 days before departure

This pre-adaptation reduces the adjustment burden upon arrival.

### 3. In-flight recovery strategy

On the plane:
- **Immediately upon boarding:** set watch to destination time zone; mentally commit to destination schedule
- **Sleep:** sleep if it is nighttime at the destination; use eye mask, earplugs, neck pillow; melatonin (0.5-3mg) if needed and compliant with destination anti-doping regulations
- **Stay awake:** if destination time is daytime upon arrival, avoid sleep on the plane (stay awake despite overnight flight); short nap (<20 min) is acceptable
- **Hydration:** drink water consistently (1 cup/hour); aircraft cabin humidity is extremely low (10-15%); dehydration accelerates jet lag symptoms
- **Avoid alcohol:** suppresses REM sleep; accelerates dehydration; worsens jet lag
- **Move:** stand and walk every 90 min to reduce DVT risk and maintain circulation; light stretching in the aisle

### 4. Post-arrival first-day protocol

Day 1 at destination is the highest-vulnerability day:

**Light exposure:** most powerful circadian synchronizer — 20-30 min of natural daylight as close to appropriate local time as possible
- Eastward: bright light exposure in the morning at destination (7-9am local time)
- Westward: avoid bright light in the early morning; seek it in the evening (5-7pm local time)

**Melatonin:** 0.5-3mg taken 30-60 min before target bedtime at destination for first 3-4 nights; most effective for eastward adjustment. Check WADA prohibited list status for the competition territory.

**Avoid naps >20 min:** short naps (10-20 min) help acute fatigue without disrupting nighttime sleep; longer naps anchor circadian rhythm to home time

**Exercise timing:** a short (30-45 min) moderate-intensity session at mid-morning local time accelerates adaptation and maintains athlete conditioning.

### 5. Manage competition timing relative to arrival

**Competing within 24 hours of arrival:** 
- Attempt to remain on home time zone during travel
- Schedule competition at the time of day that aligns with home time peak performance window
- Accept some impairment is unavoidable; focus on sleep quality, hydration, and pre-competition routine

**Competing 2-3 days after arrival:**
- Full circadian shift is not possible in 2-3 days; focus on optimal sleep timing and light exposure
- Competitive athletes show faster adaptation than non-athletes due to training-associated zeitgebers (time cues)

**Competing 5+ days after arrival:**
- Full adaptation protocol; target complete circadian shift before competition

### 6. Nutrition timing adjustment

Meal timing is a secondary circadian zeitgeber:
- Begin eating at destination meal times immediately upon arrival (even if not hungry)
- High-protein breakfast helps morning alertness; moderate carbohydrate dinner supports sleep onset
- Avoid large meals in the 2-3 hours before sleep at the destination (suppress melatonin onset)

## Common Mistakes

- **Staying on home time zone during a long trip:** For trips >4 days, staying on home time means never adapting. Embrace the local time immediately.
- **Napping without a timer:** A 90-minute nap at 2pm local time on day 1 of a new time zone anchors the circadian rhythm to the wrong time. 20-min maximum until nighttime sleep schedule is established.
- **Alcohol on the flight:** Every flight attendant offers it; every sports medicine professional advises against it for athletes.

## When NOT to Use

- Short trips (2-3 days): adaptation has not fully occurred in 2-3 days anyway; staying on home time zone is preferable for single-game travel.
