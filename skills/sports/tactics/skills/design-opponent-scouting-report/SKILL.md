# Design Opponent Scouting Report

Build a structured analysis of an opponent that surfaces actionable tactical patterns, key threats, and exploitable weaknesses — with the coaching points that translate directly into a game plan.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** Every elite team sport organization (EPL clubs, NFL franchises, NBA teams, international rugby programs) employs performance analysts to produce structured opponent scouting reports. The New Zealand All Blacks, USWNT, and Golden State Warriors coaching staff have published the use of video analysis and scouting systems as core components of preparation.
**Impact:** Hughes & Franks (2008) demonstrated that systematic performance analysis produces more accurate identification of opponent patterns than coach memory alone — coaches typically recall <60% of key match events accurately within 30 minutes of a game. Film-based scouting in the NFL has been credited as a primary driver of coaching advantage since the Walsh 49ers era. Structured scouting enables players to mentally rehearse specific opponent behaviors before the match — reducing cognitive load during competition.

## Steps

### 1. Define the scouting scope and data sources

Identify what information is available and relevant:
- **Video footage:** opponent's last 3-5 matches (more if available) against similar-level teams
- **Statistics:** possession, shots on target, pressing intensity metrics, set piece frequency, etc.
- **Live scouting:** for high-stakes matches, attend an opponent match in person
- **Historical data:** if the same opponent has been faced before, include previous match data

Scope the report to the questions that matter for your game plan — not everything about the opponent.

### 2. Analyze organizational shape and structure

For team sports, identify:
- **Defensive shape:** formation, defensive line height, man-marking vs. zonal, press triggers
- **Offensive structure:** build-up patterns from goalkeeper or defenders, progressive passing networks, wide vs. central play preference
- **Pressing behavior:** when and where they press (high press from goal kick? Press on certain triggers?)
- **Transitions:** how quickly they transition defense-to-attack and attack-to-defense

For individual sports (tennis, boxing, martial arts): replace shape analysis with stance, footwork patterns, and common set-up sequences.

### 3. Profile key personnel

Identify 3-5 key individuals who disproportionately influence the opponent:
- The primary ball carrier / creator
- The defensive anchor
- The set piece specialist (if applicable)

For each key player: strengths (do not allow), weaknesses (exploit), tendencies (turning direction, preferred foot, habitual movement patterns), and one specific defensive/offensive assignment relevant to your team.

### 4. Catalog set piece patterns

Set pieces are structured enough to be pre-programmed and are high-value in many sports:
- Offensive corners and free kicks: who delivers, where do runners go, primary and secondary targets
- Defensive set pieces: zonal vs. man-marking, who attacks the ball, second ball positioning
- Kick-offs, throw-ins, goal kicks: do they play short or long? Any patterns?

Identify 1-2 exploitable tendencies and 1-2 primary threats to defend against.

### 5. Identify exploitable weaknesses

Translate analysis into specific opportunities:
- **Spatial:** where is their defensive shape weakest? (Behind the fullbacks? Between the lines?)
- **Individual:** which opponent loses aerial duels? Which struggles defensively 1v1?
- **Transition:** are they exposed when they lose possession high up the pitch?
- **Set pieces:** which type of delivery are they weak against?

Prioritize: identify the 2-3 highest-probability exploitation opportunities, not 10.

### 6. Present the report as coaching tools

Structure the deliverable for use by coaching staff and athletes:
- **Executive summary:** 3-5 bullet points — the most important things to do and avoid
- **Video clips:** 4-8 clips showing the key patterns identified (athletes learn from seeing)
- **Defensive instructions:** specific assignments for each opponent threat
- **Offensive game plan:** 2-3 specific strategies to exploit identified weaknesses

Length matters: a 40-slide scouting report delivered 12 hours before a match is unusable. Athletes retain 2-3 key points.

### 7. Debrief players on the scouting report

Deliver the report in a team meeting:
- Show video clips to illustrate the most important patterns
- Give each player their individual defensive assignment relative to the key opponents
- Ask questions to check understanding ("If they play a through ball behind the fullback, what's your trigger?")
- Do not overwhelm — prioritize the 3 most important things for each phase of play

## Common Mistakes

- **Reporting without prescribing:** Saying "they like to play down the left" is not actionable. "Push them right and win the ball on the halfway line" is.
- **Overloading athletes:** More information does not equal better preparation. Athletes need to play on instinct; excessive information pre-match inhibits automaticity.
- **Analyzing recent matches only against weaker opposition:** Scouting from matches where the opponent dominated or struggled badly misrepresents their actual tendencies.

## When NOT to Use

- Limited preparation time (same-day match): simplify to a 5-point verbal brief on key threats.
- Developing-level competition where the opponent's pattern is less structured: focus on own team's principles instead.
