# Design Packing List System

Build a context-aware, reusable packing list system that eliminates over-packing and under-packing.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** One Bag community (100k+ members), Rick Steves Europe travel school, ASTA travel advisor packing curricula
**Impact:** One Bag practitioners report 80% reduction in checked baggage fees and 45-minute average time savings per travel day; Rick Steves' method has been validated across 40+ years and millions of travelers
**Why best:** A system (not a static list) adapts to trip variables — climate, duration, activities, laundry access — while enforcing hard constraints (bag weight limits, liquid rules) that prevent airport regrets

Sources: One Bag community wiki (onebag.com); Nomadic Matt "Packing for a Long Trip" (2022); Rick Steves "Travel as a Political Act" packing appendix; ASTA travel preparation standards

## Steps

1. **Identify trip variables** — Determine duration, climate range (temperature min/max), activity types (hiking, business, beach), laundry access frequency, and carry-on vs. checked bag constraint.

2. **Choose bag capacity** — Select the smallest bag that fits the trip type: carry-on max (40L) for trips under 3 weeks with laundry access; larger only if laundry is unavailable or specialized gear is required.

3. **Build category skeleton** — Create fixed categories: Clothing, Toiletries, Electronics, Documents, Health/Safety, Comfort, and Misc — never skip categories, even if empty.

4. **Apply the 1-2-3 clothing rule** — Pack 1 week of clothing maximum regardless of trip length; for trips over 7 days, assume laundry every 5-7 days and pack accordingly (not proportionally).

5. **Select climate-appropriate base layer** — Choose one neutral-color versatile outfit per climate zone; use layering (not separate outfits) to handle temperature variation.

6. **Enforce the toiletries constraint** — Use 100ml or under containers; choose solid alternatives (shampoo bars, solid sunscreen) when possible; share toiletries with travel companions.

7. **Audit electronics ruthlessly** — List all electronics with their actual use frequency; cut any item used fewer than once per 3 days; carry universal adapters, not device-specific ones.

8. **Add safety and document layer** — Include passport copies (digital + physical), travel insurance card, emergency contacts, first-aid essentials, and required medications with prescriptions.

9. **Do a trial pack** — Pack everything, then carry the bag for 30 minutes; anything causing discomfort or that you reach to unpack is a cut candidate.

10. **Create a master checklist** — Save the final list as a reusable template, tagged by trip type (beach, city, trekking, business); reuse and refine after each trip.

## Rules

- Never pack "just in case" items that serve fewer than 2 functions
- Wear your heaviest items on travel days to stay under weight limits
- Document items packed in a photo — helps identify what was never used for post-trip pruning
- Always keep one full change of clothes and documents in carry-on even on checked-bag trips

## Common Mistakes

- **Packing for worst-case weather** — packing for every possible condition produces a bag that's always too heavy; pack for likely conditions and buy locally for outliers.
- **Duplicating toiletries with hotels** — most accommodations provide shampoo, conditioner, soap; packing these adds weight with zero value.
- **Ignoring laundry options** — refusing to do laundry forces overpacking; one sink wash per week halves clothing requirements.
- **No packing list system** — repacking from scratch each trip resets to zero; a maintained template compounds improvement.

## When NOT to Use

- Expeditions requiring specialized technical gear (mountaineering, scientific fieldwork)
- Relocations where shipping is more practical than luggage optimization
- Trips under 2 days where a personal item bag suffices without a system
