# Design Training Program

Build a periodized resistance training program grounded in NSCA and ACSM evidence-based principles.

## Why This Is Best Practice

**Adopted by:** National Strength and Conditioning Association (NSCA), USA Weightlifting, professional sports organizations (NFL, NBA, MLB)
**Impact:** NSCA research and meta-analyses consistently show periodized training produces 10–15% greater strength gains than non-periodized training over 12 weeks; progressive overload is the single most validated principle in exercise science.

**Why best:** Periodization prevents accommodation (the body's adaptation to a fixed stimulus) by systematically varying volume, intensity, and exercise selection over time. Without structure, most trainees reach a plateau within 6–12 weeks. A written program also enables objective progress tracking and informed adjustments.

## Steps

1. **Assess the trainee** — Record training age, current 1RM (or RPE-based estimates), injury history, time available per week, equipment access, and primary goal (strength, hypertrophy, power, endurance).
2. **Set the training split** — Choose based on frequency: 2 days/week = full body; 3 days/week = full body or push-pull-legs; 4+ days/week = upper-lower or body-part split; beginners benefit from higher frequency per muscle group.
3. **Select primary exercises** — Anchor each session around 1–2 compound movements (squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press, row, pull-up); these generate the highest neuromuscular stimulus per unit time.
4. **Apply rep range to goal** — Strength: 1–5 reps at 85–100% 1RM; hypertrophy: 6–20 reps at 60–85% 1RM (any rep range with sufficient effort stimulates hypertrophy, per Schoenfeld 2017 meta-analysis); endurance: 15–25 reps.
5. **Prescribe volume** — Hypertrophy: 10–20 working sets per muscle group per week (Israetel's volume landmarks); strength: 5–10 sets per pattern per week; beginners start at the lower end.
6. **Program progressive overload** — Increase load by 2.5–5 kg on upper body and 5–10 kg on lower body when the top rep target is reached with good technique for 2 consecutive sessions (double-progression).
7. **Plan the mesocycle** — Design 4–8 week blocks; ramp volume weeks 1–3, deload week 4; repeat at higher base volume for the next mesocycle.
8. **Include warm-up and accessory work** — Each session: 5 min general warm-up + 2–3 ramp-up sets for primary lift; add 2–4 isolation exercises for weak points or injury prevention.

## Rules

- Always prioritize technique over load; a failed rep with poor form is not a training stimulus, it is injury risk.
- Never increase both volume and intensity in the same week; manipulate one variable at a time.
- Allow 48 hours minimum between sessions targeting the same muscle group.
- Beginners (< 1 year training): add weight every session (linear progression); intermediates: weekly; advanced: mesocycle-level.
- If sleep, nutrition, or life stress is severely compromised, reduce volume by 30–40%; training under excessive fatigue impairs adaptation and increases injury risk.

## Examples

**Intermediate hypertrophy, 4 days/week (Upper-Lower):** Upper A: Bench Press 4×6–8, Barbell Row 4×6–8, OHP 3×8–12, Cable Row 3×10–15, Curl/Tricep superset 3×12–15. Lower A: Squat 4×6–8, Romanian Deadlift 3×8–12, Leg Press 3×10–15, Nordic Curl 3×8–10. Upper B/Lower B: variant exercises at higher rep ranges. Deload week 4: 50% volume, 90% intensity.

## Common Mistakes

- **Program hopping** — Switching programs every 2–3 weeks prevents any single program from producing adaptation; commit to at minimum one full mesocycle (4–8 weeks).
- **Neglecting posterior chain** — Most trainees over-emphasize pushing movements; maintain at minimum a 1:1 push-to-pull ratio.
- **Skipping deloads** — Accumulated fatigue masks fitness; deloads allow supercompensation and prevent overtraining.
